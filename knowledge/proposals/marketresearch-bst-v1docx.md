## Regulatory Environment

### NDAA Compliance
- Federal law prohibits DoD from purchasing drones that:
  - Are manufactured in or by companies based in covered foreign countries, OR
  - Use components (flight controllers, radios, data transmission, cameras, gimbals, ground control systems, operating software) manufactured in/by covered foreign countries
- **Blue UAS Program:** DoD Defense Innovation Unit certification verifies NDAA compliance through supply chain security and cybersecurity assessments
- **Critical Advantage:** BST aircraft should meet NDAA compliance, eliminating DJI and other foreign-manufactured systems from federal agency use
- **Implication:** This regulatory environment creates significant market opportunity for domestically-compliant UAS manufacturers

## Wildland Fire Management Context

### UAS Classification for Wildland Fire (National Wildfire Coordinating Group)

**Type 1 & 2 (Strategic Operations)**
- Contractor-operated
- Provide strategic situational awareness and incident mapping
- Operate above other incident aircraft
- Equipped with Mode C transponders
- Maintain communications on Victor (AM) or air-to-ground (FM) frequencies
- Typical examples: ScanEagle, Aerosonde, Penguin C

**Type 3 & 4 (Tactical Operations)**
- Agency-operated
- Tactical situational awareness or mapping on/near fireline
- Most without transponders
- FM frequency communications only
- No Automated Flight Following (AFF) equipment
- Typical examples: 3DR Solo (rotary-wing), FireFly6 (fixed-wing)

### UAS Operational Advantages in Wildfire
- Superior maneuverability vs. helicopters/manned aircraft
- Can fly where manned aircraft cannot
- Low cost and flexible deployment
- High spatiotemporal resolution
- High-resolution imagery and sub-centimeter data in smoke and at night
- Real-time data without risking pilot lives
- Long-duration operations at low/slow flight profiles
- Tracking and mapping fire patterns for informed incident decisions

### Wildland Fire Incident Statistics (2022-2023)
- **2022 Incidents:** 68,988 reported wildfires (vs. 58,985 in 2021)
- **2022 Acres:** 7,577,183 acres burned (vs. 7,125,643 in 2021)
- **2022 above 10-year average:** Number of fires significantly higher; acres burned consistent with average
- **Significant Incidents:** 45 incidents over 40,000 acres (30 in Alaska)
- **Structures Lost (2022):** 2,717 total (1,261 residences, 1,294 minor structures, 91 commercial/mixed)
- **California Impact:** 492 residences, 11 commercial, 2 mixed, 252 minor structures
- **As of Sept 23, 2023:** 37 major incidents, 44,099 wildfire incidents overall
