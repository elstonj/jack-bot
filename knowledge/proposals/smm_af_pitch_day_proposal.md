## Executive Summary
Black Swift Technologies proposes to modify its existing Soil Moisture Mapping (SMM) UAS—developed in partnership with NASA for precision agriculture and scientific research—for Air Force applications. The system uses a high-resolution L-band radiometer to provide soil moisture maps at 15-meter spatial resolution down to ~10 cm depth. Phase I would establish specific Air Force customer needs, develop integration plans with Air Force weather models (particularly WRF), and document required modifications for deployment to support trafficability forecasts and runway operations.

## Technical Approach

**System Overview:**
- L-band radiometer mounted on BST's S2 UAS platform
- Provides local soil moisture measurements at 15-meter spatial resolution to ~10 cm depth
- Currently developed under NASA SBIR contract in collaboration with University of Colorado's Center for Environmental Technology (CET)
- System has been flight-tested at NASA SMAP calibration sites and commercial agriculture research farms with validation against in situ sensors

**Phase I Strategy:**
1. **Kickoff Meeting:** Establish contacts and map strategy with Air Force technical monitor
2. **Preliminary Integration Plan:** Collaborate with NOAA experts (Mimi Hughes, Rob Cifelli, Kelly Mahoney) and Professor Dev Niyogi (Purdue) to develop detailed WRF model integration plan
3. **Customer Discovery:** Reach out to 3 DoD end users to identify specific needs, focusing on Air Force forecast models and trafficability applications
4. **Requirements Documentation:** Document DoD user requirements and identify necessary instrument, CONOPS, and data structure modifications
5. **Integration Study:** Conduct detailed technical analysis to answer key questions about required resolution, accuracy, area coverage, data frequency, and format/interface requirements for seamless Air Force system integration
6. **Final Report:** Deliver comprehensive documentation for Phase II transition

**Key Research Questions to Address:**
- What resolution, accuracy, and area coverage is needed for meaningful WRF model contribution?
- What frequency and spatial coverage are required for sufficient Air Force user value?
- What are specific interfacing and format requirements for integration with existing Air Force systems?

## Products & Capabilities Described

### Soil Moisture Mapping (SMM) UAS
- **What it is:** Small unmanned aircraft system equipped with high-resolution L-band radiometer
- **Specifications:** 
  - 15-meter spatial resolution
  - ~10 cm soil moisture measurement depth
  - Lightweight airborne platform
  - On-demand, local measurement capability
- **Current Applications:** Precision agriculture, hydrological scientific studies, weather research
- **Proposed Air Force Applications:**
  - Integration into Air Force weather modeling (WRF)
  - Aircraft operations on unimproved runways (soil integrity assessment)
  - Army trafficability support
  - Battlefield environment characterization

### S2 UAS
- Airframe platform carrying the L-band radiometer payload
- Avionics developed by Dr. Elston with EMI reduction for measurement quality
- Interfaces with multiple sensors for soil moisture determination
- Flight control enabling safe near-ground operations
- Pilot-in-command operations demonstrated

## Use Cases & Applications

**Air Force:**
- Support for Air Force weather forecasts and climate analysis
- Aircraft operations on unimproved runways—soil integrity assessment for landing safety
- Integration with 557th Weather Wing (primary target customer)
- Support for both Air Force and Army weather requirements

**Army:**
- Trafficability forecasting (identified as top Army priority)
- Augmentation of existing Air Force weather data products

**Longer-term/Dual-use Applications:**
- Persistent theater soil moisture imagery via high altitude platform adaptation
- FEMA flash flood prediction (demonstrated in 2007 with older instrument version in North Texas/Oklahoma)
- Precision agriculture commercialization

**Competitive Advantage vs. Alternatives:**
- **vs. In situ probes:** Point-only measurements requiring considerable redeployment effort
- **vs. Satellites (SMAP):** Provides much higher spatial resolution (~15m vs. satellite coarse resolution) and on-demand capability vs. fixed satellite measurement cycles
