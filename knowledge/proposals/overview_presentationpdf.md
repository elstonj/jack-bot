## Executive Summary
SASCWATCH is an ONR-funded research initiative investigating air-sea coupling, waves, turbulence, and cloud formation during high-wind conditions (tropical cyclones). The project builds on decades of ONR-funded tropical cyclone research (CBLAST, ITOP) and leverages evolved observational tools (coupled numerical models, physics-resolving simulations, sUAS platforms) to address fundamental gaps in understanding surface drag coefficients, atmospheric boundary layer dynamics, and air-sea fluxes under extreme wind conditions.

## Technical Approach

**Multi-Pronged Methodology:**
1. **Observations** - Dense spatial coverage with "light" and "heavy" deployment strategies; before/during/after observations coordinated with ocean assets
2. **Simulation and Coupled Modeling** - Large-eddy simulation (LES), coupled wind/wave/ocean models (SKRIPS model), data assimilation techniques
3. **Theory and Parameterization** - Development of improved flux parameterizations based on observations and modeling results

**Key Technical Components:**
- Observational coordination between airborne platforms and ocean-based assets
- Multi-scale modeling from large-eddy simulations to coupled mesoscale models
- Leveraging NOAA APHEX modules (CHAOS, RICO SUAVE)
- Physics-resolving numerical simulations to resolve turbulent processes in the hurricane boundary layer (HBL)

## Research Questions & Hypotheses

**RQ1: How do surface waves in extreme environments modulate air-sea transfer?**
- H1a: Drag coefficient (CD) depends on wind-wave alignment, wave slope, and storm-relative position—not wind speed alone
- H1b: Heat flux coefficients (CH, CE) show compensated response to wave parameters despite wave influence on latent/sensible heat fluxes

**RQ2: Can emerging observational/numerical techniques resolve air-sea fluxes at high winds?**
- H2a: Simple flux-profile relationships inadequate; wave/swell-induced turbulence, shallow HBL, and storm-scale forcing (radial advection, pressure gradients) modify boundary layer structure
- H2b: Coherent roll structures in high-wind HBL are predictable variations of low-wind marine boundary layer phenomena

**RQ3: What is the nature of air-sea flux transitions from low-wind to TC conditions?**
- H3a: Surface currents and HBL stability—typically neglected at high winds—produce non-negligible impacts on flux estimates
- H3b: Sharp transition identifiable where wind-wave interactions dominate
