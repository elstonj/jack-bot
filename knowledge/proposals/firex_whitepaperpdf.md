## Executive Summary
FIREX (Fire Influence on Regional and Global Environments Experiment) is a comprehensive NOAA research program (2015-2019) to understand and predict the impact of North American biomass burning on atmospheric chemistry, air quality, and climate. The program combines laboratory experiments, field measurements using multiple platforms (NOAA WP-3 aircraft, mobile lab, small aircraft, ground stations), and modeling to address critical unknowns about wildfire emissions, smoke transformation, and regional/global impacts.

## Technical Approach

### Research Strategy (Multi-Year, Phased)
1. **Instrument & Methods Development (FY2015-2019)**: Development of new mass spectrometry techniques (H3O⁺CIMS, I-CIMS, nitrate-CIMS, 2D-GC-MS, TAG-MS) for identifying previously unidentified compounds; broadband extinction measurements for brown carbon; photoacoustic methods for black carbon lensing effects; aerosol electrospray mass spectrometry.

2. **Laboratory Studies**: 
   - Fire Science Laboratory (USDA Fire Lab, Missoula, Montana) for emission factor measurements from typical North American fuels
   - CIRES atmospheric simulation chamber experiments at CU Boulder for chemical transformation studies
   - Identification of unknown semi-volatile organic compounds (SVOCs), intermediate-volatility organic compounds (IVOCs), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs)

3. **Small-Scale Field Experiments (FY2016-2017)**:
   - Ground site measurements at Storm Peak Laboratory, Colorado (high-elevation site sensitive to regional BB emissions)
   - Mobile laboratory deployments for ground-level and nighttime smoke sampling
   - Small aircraft (Twin Otter or similar) for near-field emission studies
   - Unmanned aerial systems (UAS) for meteorological parameters and aerosol measurements

4. **Large-Scale Intensive Field Campaign (Summer 2018)**:
   - NOAA WP-3D aircraft as primary platform (boundary layer to middle troposphere, regional scale)
   - Coordinated deployment of multiple small aircraft, mobile laboratory, and ground-based systems
   - Multi-platform simultaneous measurements (day and night)
   - Satellite observation integration
   - Deployment bases chosen based on seasonal fire locations (Pacific Northwest targeting August activity)

5. **Post-Campaign Analysis (FY2019)**: Fire lab and chamber studies using fuels from measured fires; interpretation and synthesis across platforms.

### Platforms & Instrumentation
- **NOAA WP-3D aircraft**: Primary airborne platform for medium-to-regional scale chemical/physical characterization
- **Twin Otter or similar small aircraft**: Near-field emission studies, temporal evolution of emissions
- **Mobile laboratory**: Ground-level, nighttime smoke measurements; near-source observations inaccessible to aircraft
- **UAS**: Remote measurements of meteorological parameters, particle number density, CO/CO₂
- **Ground stations**: Storm Peak Laboratory for long-term, dilute aged smoke characterization
- **New instrumentation**: H3O⁺CIMS, I-CIMS, nitrate-CIMS, 2D-GC-MS, TAG-MS, broadband extinction photometry, photoacoustic instruments

## Use Cases & Applications

### Primary Mission: Wildfire & Biomass Burning Research
1. **Emissions Characterization**:
   - Fuel-specific emission factors (modified combustion efficiency dependent)
   - Identification of unidentified VOCs/SVOCs and greenhouse gas emissions (CH₄, N₂O)
   - Trace gases (NOₓ, CO, CO₂) and aerosol composition (BC, BrC, OC, mineral dust)
   - Air toxics and reduced nitrogen compounds (HONO, isocyanic acid)

2. **Chemical Transformation**:
   - Secondary organic aerosol (SOA) formation mechanisms
   - Ozone production in fire plumes (daytime photooxidation)
   - Nighttime chemistry (NO₃, N₂O₅-driven processing, ClNO₂ formation)
   - Peroxyacetyl nitrate (PAN) formation and stability
   - Particulate matter aging, coagulation, volatility equilibration

3. **Air Quality Impacts**:
   - Local ozone and particulate pollution prediction
   - Visibility impairment
   - Population exposure to fire toxics and particulates
   - Prescribed burn and management strategy optimization

4. **Climate Impacts**:
   - Black carbon and brown carbon optical properties (absorption, scattering)
   - Aerosol optical depth (AOD) contributions
   - Cloud condensation nuclei (CCN) and ice nucleation properties
   - Radiative forcing (heating vs. cooling effects)
   - Global distribution and long-range transport of BB aerosols

5. **Geographic Focus**:
   - Western North America (Pacific Northwest, California, Montana)
   - Arctic impacts (Asian fire contributions)
   - Southeast U.S. agricultural burning
   - Regional and global atmospheric transport pathways

### Policy-Relevant Applications
- Fire management decision support (prescribed burn timing, suppression strategy)
- Air quality waivers and NAAQS (National Ambient Air Quality Standards) compliance
- Health advisory systems
- Ecosystem and urban-wildland interface management
- Climate adaptation strategies for increased wildfire frequency

## Key Scientific Questions Addressed

1. **Emissions**: What are the complete gas, aerosol, and greenhouse gas emissions from North American fires under various fuel and meteorological conditions?

2. **Chemical Transformation**: How do fire emissions evolve chemically and physically during transport and aging over hours to days?

3. **Local Air Quality**: How well can we predict BB impact on ozone, particulates, visibility, and health outcomes?

4. **Regional/Long-Term Impacts**: How far and with what persistence do BB emissions affect air quality and atmospheric composition across North America?

5. **Climate Properties**: What are the optical, radiative, and cloud-interaction properties of BB aerosols, and how are they best represented in climate models?

## Notable Details & Context

### Scientific Motivation
- Western U.S. wildfire frequency and severity projected to increase 2-4× per degree of warming
- 20th-century fire suppression created fuel buildup, ecosystem damage, and underestimated urban-wildland interface risks
- Previous campaigns (ICARTT 2004, ARCPAC 2008, SENEX 2013, ARCTAS 2008, DC3/SEAC4RS 2012-2013, BBOP 2012, FLAME-4 laboratory) provided foundational data but left critical gaps, particularly in nighttime processing and regional/global integration

### Key Scientific Gaps Identified
1. **Unidentified emissions**: ~20-50% of VOCs/SVOCs remain chemically unidentified; unidentified SVOCs have disproportionate impact on SOA prediction
2. **Nighttime chemistry**: Almost entirely under-sampled despite evidence of "prodigious" nighttime smoke production (e.g., 2013 Rim Fire)
3. **Emission inventory validation**: Satellites undercount small fires and domestic burning; need ground-truth from airborne measurements
4. **Particulate optical properties**: Complex interplay of black carbon "lensing" by organic coatings, brown carbon absorption, and relative BC/OC ratios poorly understood
5. **Chemical aging mechanisms**: Complexity of ozone formation in fire plumes (sometimes occurs, sometimes not) unexplained; heterogeneous particle chemistry not well characterized

### Novel FIREX Contributions
- **Multi-platform simultaneous sampling**: First coordinated deployment combining WP-3D (regional), Twin Otter (near-field), mobile lab (ground/night), UAS, and satellites
- **Nighttime focus**: Dedicated mobile laboratory deployment for ground-hugging nighttime smoke, which is most relevant to local air quality despite being least studied
- **New analytical capabilities**: Previously unavailable mass spectrometry techniques for organic compound speciation
- **Real smoke chamber studies**: CIRES facility allows injection of actual fire smoke into aging chambers (not just synthetic mixtures)
- **Aerosol property-satellite connection**: Direct measurement of cloud/climate-relevant optical properties (angle-dependent scattering, hygroscopic growth) tied to satellite observations
