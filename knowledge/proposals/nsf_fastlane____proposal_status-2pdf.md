## Executive Summary
This NSF STTR Phase I proposal sought to develop a small unmanned aircraft system (sUAS) for in-situ measurement of atmospheric boundary layer parameters to support monitoring and forecasting of thunderstorms, hurricanes, and fire weather. The proposal received a "Very Good" rating from the review panel, which praised the technical approach and team expertise while noting concerns about cost estimation and endurance challenges with all-electric systems.

## Technical Approach
- Development of a small UAS platform with autopilot control capabilities
- In-situ sensor suite for atmospheric parameter measurement
- Systems intended to operate in boundary layer sampling missions
- All-electric power configuration proposed
- Integration of GPS and multiple sensors for automated flight and data collection

## Products & Capabilities Described
**Small UAS (sUAS) Platform:**
- Intended for atmospheric boundary layer sampling
- All-electric powered
- Autopilot control system
- Low-cost and reusable platform design
- Capability to carry multiple atmospheric sensors

## Use Cases & Applications
- Thunderstorm monitoring and forecasting
- Hurricane observation and characterization
- Fire weather assessment
- Routine atmospheric parameter sampling for meteorological research
- Weather forecasting support

## Review Assessment

**Strengths (Reviewer Noted):**
1. PI (Jack Elston) has considerable experience in sUAS development and testing
2. Strong subcontractor team (Profs. Argrow and Lawrence) providing expertise and facilities
3. Clear proposal documentation of development plan and technical details
4. Broad range of applications and large potential market if successful

**Weaknesses (Reviewer Noted):**
1. **No cost estimate provided** - despite low-cost being a key advantage claim; actual systems with proposed capabilities (autopilot, GPS, radar, multiple sensors) likely to exceed budget
2. **Endurance challenges** - achieving long flight duration with all-electric power while carrying proposed sensor suite may be technically difficult
