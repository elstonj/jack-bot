# Earth Science Applications: Disaster Risk Reduction and Response

## Document Metadata
- Type: NASA ROSES Program Solicitation / Call for Proposals
- Client/Agency: NASA Earth Science Division (ESD), Applied Sciences Program
- Program/Solicitation: ROSES-2018, Program Element A.37
- Date: June 1, 2018 (Amended); Original April 10, 2018
- BST Products/Systems Referenced: None
- Key Personnel: None named (this is a NASA solicitation, not a BST proposal)

## Executive Summary
This is a NASA solicitation for mature applications projects (ARL ≥ 4) that apply Earth observations to improve decision-making in disaster risk reduction and response. The program seeks user-centric research that will transition proven applications to sustained operational status with end-user organizations, with mandatory cost-sharing requirements escalating from 0% in Year 1 to 60% in Year 4. Projects must form Disaster Application Response and Recovery Teams (DARRTs) and be prepared to support NASA's disaster response activities.

## Program Objectives
- Develop and demonstrate improvements to decision-making activities using Earth observations
- Transfer and enable adoption of applications by end-user organizations sustainably
- Create an integrated "toolbox" of experimental disaster management applications
- Advance Application Readiness Levels (ARLs) from initial proof-of-concept to operational deployment
- Support disaster preparedness, mitigation, response, and recovery phases

## Eligible Hazard Types and Disaster Themes
**Primary Hazards** (at least one required):
- Hydrological: floods, inundation (coastal, riverine, floodplain, glacial)
- Geological: earthquakes, tsunamis, landslides, volcanoes
- Atmospheric: tropical cyclones, severe weather, volcanic aerosols
- Ecological: wildfires, algal blooms
- Technological: oil spills, chemical releases, marine debris

**Secondary/Cascading Hazards**: Infrastructure damage, landslides, tsunamis, volcanic eruptions, storm surge, forest fires, supply chain failures

**Environmental Contexts**:
- Coastal, river basin, seismic zones, rural, high mountain, urban
- Lives and safety, infrastructure, economies

## Technical Approach Requirements

**Earth Observations Integration**:
- Must incorporate NASA satellite missions (current and planned constellation)
- Airborne assets and International Space Station observations
- Ground-based stations and geodetic data
- Non-NASA data acceptable if used with NASA data or as proxies
- Multi-sensor data fusion (optical, microwave, radar, LIDAR, SAR, GNSS)
- Commercial satellite data permitted if meets technical requirements

**Application Development**:
- Risk assessment, risk-based prediction, and monitoring tools
- Incident and emergency response capabilities
- Damage assessment and recovery mapping
- Resilience and adaptation strategies
- Decision support systems for situational awareness
- Modeling and mapping to improve response and recovery planning

**Decision-Making Focus**:
- User-centric: must address specific decision-making activities of identified end-users
- Applications should improve speed (lower latency), accuracy, and reliability of information
- Must balance precision against practical utility for responders
- Tools must be interoperable and integrable into existing decision systems

## Products & Capabilities Required

Projects must deliver:
1. **Validated Decision Support Tools** including:
   - Risk and resilience assessment maps and models
   - Impact extent and damage maps
   - Recovery and restoration analysis products
   - Early warning systems (where applicable)

2. **Data and Information Products**:
   - Analysis-ready, freely accessible data with metadata
   - Risk-based monitoring systems
   - Crisis informatics tools
   - Geospatial products compatible with standard platforms and web services

3. **Operational Capabilities**:
   - Performance measures and quality assurance procedures
   - Thresholds for accuracy and reliability scientifically valid but balanced for operational use
   - Latency requirements documented and validated

## Use Cases & Applications
- **Preparedness**: Baseline condition monitoring, risk assessment, hazard scenario planning
- **Mitigation**: Vulnerability assessment, resilience building, infrastructure protection
- **Response**: Real-time situational awareness, damage assessment, resource allocation
- **Recovery**: Damage mapping, restoration monitoring, rebuilding guidance

**Geographic Scope Requirements**:
- Projects must begin with specific geographical focus but be globally extensible
- Must address trans-boundary challenges where applicable
- Multi-national, national, regional, tribal, state, and sub-state projects eligible
- International projects must include established organizations with international mandate

## Key Evaluation Criteria

**Relevance**:
1. What application science question is being addressed?
2. What is at risk (people, ecosystems, infrastructure) and why is this important?
3. What worst-case disaster scenario does the project aim to avoid and why is it urgent?
4. What is the purpose of resilience efforts and why will results be durable?
5. Who are vested stakeholders and why are they committed to partnership?

**Intrinsic Merit**:
- Ability to develop, test, demonstrate, and transition the application
- Quality of Earth observations approach and methodology
- Clear characterization of decision-making needs and improvements
- Quality of multidisciplinary teaming and end-user involvement
- Path to sustained transition and operational adoption

**Cost Reasonableness**:
- Overall project management approach and feasibility
- Cost-effective achievement of objectives
- Performance measures and tracking plan
- Feasibility of sustained results post-project

## Cost-Sharing Requirements

**Base Funding Schedule** (mandatory escalation):
- **Year 1**: NASA 100%, Partner 0% (feasibility/proof-of-concept, ARL advancement)
- **Year 2**: NASA 80%, Partner 20% (ARL ≥ 5)
- **Year 3**: NASA 60%, Partner 40% (continued development)
- **Year 4**: NASA 40%, Partner 60% (transition to operations, ARL 8-9)

**Incentive Funding** (optional, up to 50% match):
- Additional NASA matching up to 50% of partner contributions exceeding requirements
- Subject to caps: Year 1 ($50K NASA, $100K partner max); Year 2 ($100K NASA, $200K partner); Year 3 ($150K NASA, $300K partner); Year 4 ($100K NASA, $200K partner)

**Cost-Sharing Requirements**:
- Preferred: financial contributions
- Allowed: in-kind contributions (must be certified and valued)
- Partner organizations must demonstrate growing commitment as project matures
- End-users assume operational costs post-project
- Cannot use Federal awards to meet matching requirements (exception: non-transfer partnership agreements with Federal agencies)

## Disaster Application Response and Recovery Teams (DARRTs)

**DARRT Structure**:
- Projects aggregate into thematic teams addressing one or more disaster types
- Multi-organizational, multidisciplinary, multi-sectoral composition encouraged
- May include 2 Co-PIs: one for technical leadership, one for decision-making/application aspects
- Members receive training on NASA Disaster Response Playbooks (flood, earthquake, severe storm)

**DARRT Participation Requirements**:
- All funded projects must be active DARRT participants
- Best-effort participation in NASA disaster responses when events trigger activation
- Testing, validation, and integration of experimental products during real-world responses
- Scenario-based exercises and pilot projects engagement
- Quarterly ARL advancement assessments required
- Contribution tracking for resource effectiveness evaluation

**DARRT Participation Plan Components** (required in proposals):
- Latencies (response timing requirements)
- Geospatial and temporal scales of coverage
- Socio-economic and cultural factors
- Accuracy and reliability thresholds
- Extensibility across disaster types

## Partner Organization Requirements

**End-User Involvement** (mandatory):
- Identified organizations must have decision-making authority and responsibility
- Must be involved from project onset throughout ARL advancement
- Must be earnestly committed to transition and sustained adoption
- Growing resource commitments expected as application matures
- Must provide feedback during testing and validation

**Partner Engagement Plan** (required in proposal):
- Stakeholder identification and collaboration structure
- End-user and practitioner involvement strategy
- Capacity building and training approach
- Network development across science, technology, and disaster risk sectors
- Clear path for partnership expansion and transfer opportunities

**Eligible Partners**:
- Government agencies
- Non-governmental organizations
- Private sector companies (especially insurance, infrastructure, risk management)
- Emergency management and resource planning organizations
- International organizations and development bodies
- Humanitarian organizations
- Tribal organizations
- Communities and vulnerable populations

## Application Readiness Level (ARL) Expectations

**Program Focus**: Mature applications at ARL ≥ 4 (non-compliant if ARL 1-3)

**Advancement Timeline**:
- Must achieve **ARL ≥ 5 within 12-18 months** (Year 1)
- Must reach **ARL 6-9 within remaining 3-year timeframe**
- Quarterly assessment of PI and end-user partners required
- Bimonthly/annual project reviews with Program Manager
- Site meetings may be required

**ARL Advancement Mechanisms**:
- Deployment and testing in relevant environments
- NASA disaster response activations (real-world testing)
- Incorporation into Disaster Response Playbooks
- Scenario exercises and pilot projects

## Transition and Sustainability Plan

**Must Address**:
1. End-user/partner financial sustainability (budget for implementation)
2. End-user/partner organizational sustainability (technical staff)
3. End-user/partner technological sustainability (infrastructure/IT)
4. Transition plan and timeline from research to operational status

**Post-Project Responsibility**:
- Partner organizations responsible for operational costs post-project
- NASA continues providing Earth observations through data centers as possible
- Final year includes transition activities and end-of-project announcement event

## Project Duration and Phasing

**Initial Phase**:
- Feasibility Phase: 16-18 months duration
- Project Review before continuation
- Satisfactory progress leads to continuation or re-alignment with DARRT
- Tasks may be augmented or reduced per reviewer recommendations

**Subsequent Phases**:
- Projects continue toward ARL 6-9 goals
- Obligation and cost plans aligned with government Fiscal Year calendar
- Total project duration up to 4 years (shorter durations allowed if objectives achievable)
- Chronic costing delinquency or non-compliance may result in termination

## Data and Information Management

**Data Standards**:
- Freely open and timely access to data required
- Analysis-ready data with comprehensive metadata
- Easily discoverable data products
- Consistent with standard government and commercial practices
- Urgent disaster and humanitarian use prioritized
- Data discovery, distribution, and management tools encouraged

**Platforms and Interoperability**:
- Standard platforms and existing visualization/geospatial services encouraged
- Compatibility with GeoPlatform architecture required
- FGDC interoperability and GIRA compliance recommended
