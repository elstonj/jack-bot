# Expendable Air-sea Profiling Observations in Hazardous Weather Conditions

## Document Metadata
- Type: STTR Solicitation/Topic Notice
- Client/Agency: Office of Naval Research (ONR), U.S. Navy
- Program/Solicitation: Navy STTR 25.A, Topic N25A-T025
- Date: Pre-release 12/4/24; Opens 1/8/25; Closes 2/5/25
- BST Products/Systems Referenced: None (this is a solicitation document, not a BST proposal)
- Key Personnel: Joshua Cossuth and James Doyle (Navy TPOCs)

## Executive Summary
The Navy seeks development of an improved, hardened expendable profiling system to capture atmospheric observations in hazardous weather conditions (tropical cyclones, polar lows, heavy convection) with better vertical, horizontal, and temporal resolution than standard radiosondes. The desired system should be compact, deployable, cost-competitive, and capable of providing higher-quality air-sea interface observations while advancing beyond current radiosonde capabilities in spatial/temporal coverage, sampling rate, durability, or cost.

## Technical Opportunity
This STTR solicitation targets development of a novel expendable in-situ profiling platform that improves upon legacy radiosonde technology for meteorological and oceanographic (METOC) observations in challenging environments. Key improvement areas sought include:

- **Spatial observations** beyond standard radiosonde networks
- **Temporal observations** and sampling rate improvements
- **Price per unit** reduction compared to radiosondes
- **Durability in hazardous conditions** (tropical cyclones, polar lows, sea ice zones)
- **Uncertainty characterization** and measurement accuracy
- **Targeting/colocation capability** with other observational platforms

The solicitation emphasizes development of a "compact, easily deployable, and competitively priced alternative" with potential to produce dozens or hundreds of units during validation and scale to larger operational production.

## Measurement Parameters & Performance Requirements
Proposed systems should measure:
- Temperature and humidity profiles
- Wind profiles
- Surface drag and flux parameters
- Dynamics and thermodynamics influenced by air-sea interactions

Success criteria include:
- Success rate matching current operational radiosonde technology
- Demonstrated improved performance in specific hazardous weather conditions
- Reduced failure rates compared to radiosondes
- Real-time deployment and tracking capability
- Data compatible with operational forecast models

## Phase Structure

**Phase I** (Feasibility):
- Background review of current observing platforms, measurement uncertainties, and logistical challenges
- Design of improved measurement concepts addressing identified limitations
- Comparative analysis with radiosondes and remote sensing (including uncertainty specifications)
- Deliverables: state-of-science report, comparison of current concepts of operations, proposed engineering/hardware/software upgrades with cost/longevity/sampling rate/coverage analysis

**Phase II** (Development & Validation):
- Fabrication and demonstration of expendable profiling prototypes
- Calibration and validation in hazardous weather conditions
- Multiple development spirals and real-world demonstrations
- Leverage existing ground/ship/aircraft delivery mechanisms for comparative validation
- Data ingest and assimilation into operational forecast models
- Iterative refinement of measurement aspects and concept of operations

**Phase III** (Commercialization):
- Technology maturation into operational expendable profiling platform
- Calibration and validation against current operational METOC observations (in-situ platforms, remote sensing)
- Real-time deployment and tracking from ground, ship, and aircraft
- Regular production of well-characterized sensors
- Operational deployment in field missions for air-sea profiling observations

## Use Cases & Applications
- **Tropical cyclone observations** (hurricanes, typhoons)
- **Polar low monitoring** in marginal ice zones
- **Heavy convective precipitation** measurement
- **Large wave event profiling**
- **Sea ice formation and break-up** monitoring
- **Complex air-sea interaction** characterization

## Operational Context
Current challenges being addressed:
- Insufficient quantity of observations due to high cost per unit
- Quality degradation in hazardous weather environments
- Operational METOC agencies rely heavily on legacy profiling techniques
- Difficulty observing and constraining remote, hazardous environments in forecast systems
- Need for improved understanding of surface drag, fluxes, and dynamic/thermodynamic properties in extreme weather

## Notable Details
- This is an STTR topic (not SBIR), requiring small business partnerships with research institutions
- Production capability during validation phase is a key evaluation criterion
- Technology must integrate with existing operational systems (ground, ship, aircraft platforms) for ease of transition
- Comparative validation against radiosondes and remote sensing is mandatory
- Real-time data transmission and operational forecast integration required
- Dual-use applications emphasized for Phase III commercialization

## Key References Provided
Literature emphasizes recent advances in:
- Expendable Digital Dropsondes (XDDs) in tropical cyclones
- Small Unmanned Aircraft Systems (sUAS) for hurricane observation
- Emerging autonomous observing technologies in Arctic regions
- Recent advancements in aircraft and in-situ tropical cyclone observations
- Uncrewed aerial vehicles with winch systems for oceanographic profiling
- Validation and traceability of miniaturized multi-parameter radiosondes
