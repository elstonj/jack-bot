## Executive Summary
NASA solicits proposals for unpiloted aerial platforms (both lighter and heavier-than-air) capable of operating in extreme Earth science environments such as volcanoes, storms, and wildfires—missions too dangerous for piloted aircraft. Current UAS platforms lack the capability to penetrate these environments reliably due to structural limitations, weather sensitivity, and icing concerns.

## Mission Requirements & Challenges

### Target Applications
1. **Volcano Penetration**: Flights into volcano plumes to measure SO₂ concentrations and compare with satellite data
2. **Storm Penetration**: UAS operations in thunderstorms and hurricanes to collect air pressure, wind conditions, temperatures for weather forecasting and model development
3. **Wildfire Operations**: Aerial platforms to gather emissions and fire behavior data

### Key Operational Challenges Identified
- Strong winds in extreme environments ground smaller UAS platforms
- Turbulence causes vehicle upset, loss of control, and structural damage
- Most UAS platforms lack weather resistance for visible precipitation operations
- Icing in extremely cold/polar conditions causes vehicle loss
- Current platforms can operate *near* extreme environments but cannot reliably *penetrate* them
- High probability of vehicle loss with existing platforms in these conditions

## Proposed Vehicle Requirements
- Must carry scientific instrumentation payload on all science missions
- Should address operational challenges to minimize vehicle loss
- Can be based on new design or extensively modified existing platforms
- Primary focus on vehicle design, but also address: concept of operations, vehicle maintenance, transport/deployment, ground station design, flight test planning

## Phase I & II Expectations

### Phase I Deliverables
- System-level design
- Proof-of-concept experiment on one or more key components/enabling technologies
- Final report describing concept analysis results and key component technology demonstrations

### Phase II Deliverables
- Concept prototype and feasibility testing
- Final report with design documentation
- Test reports
- Photographs of prototypes built and tested

## Technology Focus Areas
- TRL target: 2 to 6 at project completion
- Primary Technology Taxonomy: TX 04 Robotics Systems / TX 04.2 Mobility

## Technologies Suggested for Development
Per NASA ARMD and SBIR programs:
- Icing detection and removal systems
- Gust load alleviation
- Upset prevention, detection, and recovery
- See-and-avoid systems
- Beyond visual line of sight (BVLOS) operation technologies

## Strategic Context & Relevance
- Increased priority due to climate change effects: more frequent/intense wildfires, severe weather, long-term drought
- Government interest in developing domestic UAS manufacturing base to reduce reliance on foreign manufacturers (e.g., DJI)
- Security concerns with foreign UAS platforms
- Recent regulatory advances (NASA UAS in NAS program, UTM Project) have made UAS operations in National Airspace simpler and safer
- Emerging pool of small, innovative domestic UAS manufacturers ready to develop science-focused platforms

## Notable Details
- Emphasis on "dull, dirty, difficult, and dangerous" mission capability
- Simpler UAS platforms valued for rapid deployment to events of interest
- Private industry and multiple government agencies showing increased interest in UAS for Earth science
- RFP encourages both heavier-than-air (fixed/rotary wing) and lighter-than-air (balloon/airship) platforms
