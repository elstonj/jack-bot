## Executive Summary
Black Swift Technologies proposes to develop and commercialize the S2 Methane Leak Detection (MLD) system—a UAS-based platform integrating a laser spectrometer sensor from Quanta 3 (Colorado-based) with BST's S2 fixed-wing aircraft and sensor-directed flight control avionics. The system can survey 100+ well sites per day for methane leaks at <$35 per site (vs. $500+ for competing methods) with ppb-level detection sensitivity and precise geolocation. BST requests $250K in AIA grant funding (with $750K in matching NASA funds already awarded) to accelerate product development, field testing, customer acquisition, and commercial launch, targeting PDC Energy as an anchor customer and projecting $19M in methane detection revenue by 2025.

## Technical Approach

**Core Technology:**
- Ultra-low-mass laser spectrometer sensor (ppb detection sensitivity, <10 SCFH leak rate detection limit) co-developed with Quanta 3
- Integrated into BST's S2 fixed-wing UAS platform
- Sensor-directed flight control avionics that combine methane readings with 3D wind sensors to characterize plumes in real-time and locate leak sources
- Advanced post-processing algorithms to create actionable data within hours of flight
- Capability to estimate leak size for prioritization

**Competitive Advantages:**
- Detection capability: <10 SCFH vs. >300 SCFH for satellites/manned aircraft; ppb sensitivity vs. 200 SCFH for passive detectors
- Cost efficiency: $35/well vs. $200-$500+ for alternatives
- Superior plume characterization and leak localization vs. OGI, hyperspectral, and multispectral cameras
- Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS) operational capability (S2 can operate ~20 miles from operator)
- Robust performance in high winds and turbulence
- ~10x range/speed advantage over quadcopter drones

**Development Status:**
- Laser spectrometer bench-tested and integrated into S2
- Initial test flights completed
- Field testing planned at METEC (Methane Emissions Technology Evaluation Center) at Colorado State University
- S2 aircraft platform is mature; no further development needed for baseline aircraft
- Sensor-directed flight control is a NASA-funded development initiative
