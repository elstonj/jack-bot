## Executive Summary
This report documents the brightness temperature mapping algorithm used for the Lobe Differencing Correlation Radiometer (LDCR) during successful flight tests conducted on the Tempest UAS at the Canton Oklahoma Soilscape site on September 8-9, 2015. The algorithm implements calibration methods and antenna temperature-to-brightness temperature conversion using both N-sample grid-cell averaging and Linear Minimum Mean Square Error (LMMSE) estimation techniques to map soil moisture variations.

## Technical Approach

### Calibration Method
- **Four-state calibration system**: The LDCR uses four calibration states with differential temperature inputs (ΔT0-ΔT3) generated by internal resistive RF loads with thermoelectric cooler/heater control
- **Temperature compensation model**: Offset (Voo) and gain parameters (G0-G3) modeled as second-order polynomial functions of LDCR receiver temperature (TL) with 6 unknown parameters per calibration state
- **Estimation technique**: Simulated annealing optimization used to identify unknown parameters; least-square error (LSE) minimization with design matrix inversion
- **Pre-flight calibration data**: Fresh water calibration performed with thermistor temperature measurement; 1,180 calibration cycles analyzed (N=1,180, M=6 parameters, 1,174 degrees of freedom)
- **Antenna radiation efficiency**: MiCo antenna efficiency calculated at 95.58% (±~1% accuracy) via HFSS simulation; 3 dB beam width ~45°

### Brightness Temperature Mapping Methods

**Method 1: N-Sample Grid-Cell Averaging**
- Two-level averaging: first within each calibration cycle (N1=6 samples per cycle after dropping 5 transition samples), then across calibration cycles within grid cells
- Measurement noise std dev after first averaging: σTA,1 = σTA/√(N1) = ~2.1 K
- Measurement noise std dev after second averaging: σTA,2 = σTA,1/√(N2) ~0.9 K (N2 ~31 cycles per grid cell)
- Total samples per pixel: N = N1 × N2 = 186
- Low-pass video filter time constant: τ = 1.5 ms; A/D sampling interval: T = 1 ms
- Single-sample TA std dev: ~7.5 K (measured)

**Method 2: Unbiased LMMSE (Linear Minimum Mean Square Error)**
- Geostatistical kriging approach using radially-symmetric covariance functions
- Covariance model: C(r) = σ²[1 - (r/a)²]exp(-r²/a²) where a = 58 m (twice flight-line separation)
- Lagrange multiplier constraint ensures unbiased estimation
- Data variance: 65.61 K² (Sept 8), 62.41 K² (Sept 9)
- Covariance search radius: half-pixel radius around each grid point to ensure local mean consistency
- Unbiased LMMSE error is larger than biased variant (positive semidefinite guarantee)

### Calibration Results
- Chi-square analysis shows good fit to measured data (χ² values 1132-1298 vs. expected N-M=1174)
- Gain coefficient g00 ~2× larger than g10, g20, g30 (≈3 dB difference attributed to RF losses in quadrature hybrid and coaxial cables)
- Small asymmetries observed between mirror-symmetric states (g10 ≠ g20) suspected due to measurement noise or antenna temperature variations during calibration

## Products & Capabilities Described

### LDCR (Lobe Differencing Correlation Radiometer)
- **Purpose**: Passive microwave radiometric measurement of upwelling brightness temperature
- **Configuration**: Dual-polarization (H and V) system with four internal calibration states
- **Antenna**: MiCo antenna pairs with calculated nadir brightness temperatures of 64.4 K (H-pol) and 132.2 K (V-pol) over 23°C fresh water
- **Measurement characteristics**:
  - Radiometric sampling interval: ~1 ms
  - Calibration cycle period: ~44 ms
  - Output: Four voltage states (V0-V3) with temperature-dependent gains
  - Temperature monitoring: Four thermistors on local oscillator, isolator, IF filter, correlator chip
  - IR sensor: Surface physical temperature measurement capability
- **Performance at Canton flights**:
  - Single-sample noise: ~7.5 K
  - Grid-cell averaged noise (186 samples): ~0.9 K
  - Physical temperature measurement std dev: 0.79 K (Sept 8), 0.16 K (Sept 9)

### Tempest UAS
- **Mission profile**: Serpentine flight pattern mapping (~41-acre rectangular area)
- **Specifications**: Nominal altitude h = 35 m, flight speed v = 21 m/s
- **Mission duration**: ~16 minutes per flight
- **Navigation data rate**: ~22 ms intervals (interpolated to 1 ms radiometric grid)
- **Flight lines**: E-W separation ~29 m

## Use Cases & Applications

### Primary Application: Soil Moisture Mapping
- **Test site**: Canton Oklahoma Soilscape site (36.00°N, -98.63°W)
- **Surface characteristics**: Dry green grass (~7 inches height on Sept 8; lightly moistened with dew on Sept 9)
- **Mapping area**: ~41 acres with 12 in-situ wireless soil moisture sensor nodes (3 depths each: 5 cm, 15 cm, 30 cm)
- **Soil types**: Mollisols (9 nodes) and Inceptisols (3 nodes)
- **Key findings**:
  - Brightness temperature 20 K higher at 11:35 AM (Sept 8) vs. 8:00 AM (Sept 9)
  - Repeatable warming signature (~40 K amplitude) in NW quadrant across both days
  - Localized cooling (~40 K) in SW corner
  - Strong correlation between higher volumetric soil moisture (VSM) and lower upwelling antenna temperature

**Environmental conditions during tests**:
- Sept 8: 60% cloud cover, 27°C ambient, surface temp 29.91°C (σ=0.79°C)
- Sept 9: 100% cloud cover, 20°C ambient, surface temp 19.87°C (σ=0.16°C)
- No precipitation observed

## Key Results

### Calibration Validation
- Linear parametric model successfully captures LDCR output voltage relationships across four calibration states
- Temperature compensation model accurate to within χ² uncertainty bounds
- LDCR receiver components showed 0.5-1 K temperature rise during ~50 s calibration process

### Brightness Temperature Maps
- Two mapping methods (N-sample averaging and LMMSE) produced consistent spatial patterns
- LMMSE method provides spatially smoother estimates with quantified error covariance
- In-situ VSM measurements validate physical interpretation: lower VSM → higher antenna temperature (lower soil emissivity)
- Spatial variations in antenna temperature (±40 K) correlate with soil moisture variations, not vegetation density changes

### Algorithm Performance
- N-sample averaging method: effective noise reduction from 7.5 K (single sample) to ~0.9 K (grid cell)
- LMMSE unbiased estimation error: higher than biased variant but ensures mean value preservation
- Grid cell size determined by antenna 3 dB beam width: ~28 m at 35 m altitude

## Notable Details

### Technical Challenges Identified
- **Calibration matrix conditioning**: Design matrix poorly conditioned; simulated annealing used as alternative to SVD/Tikhonov regularization (not yet implemented)
- **Surface temperature measurement noise**: Sporadic IR sensor noise filtered using median filter (window=3)
- **Antenna temperature variations during calibration**: Small differences in mirror-symmetric states (g10 ≠ g20) suspected from land/operator side-lobe contamination
- **Model refinements pending**:
  - Time-deconvolution algorithm (provided by PI Gasiewski) to invert blurring effects and transition to level 0.2 data
  - Antenna radiation pattern effects on elevation angle (TB variation with angle not yet characterized; assumed constant for downwelling atmospheric temps)
  - Lagrangian constraint implementation for unbiased LMMSE

### Future Work Planned
- Implement time-deconvolution for low-pass filter and calibration transition effects
- Revise LMMSE covariance function based on actual MiCo antenna radiation pattern
- Comparison with satellite brightness temperature data
- Physical temperature mapping using same kriging algorithms
- Soil moisture retrieval implementation using Jackson et al. ω-τ algorithm

### Atmospheric/Environmental Assumptions
- Downwelling atmospheric brightness temperatures: assumed 2.73 K (requires 2-3 K correction for atmospheric radiation)
- Third and fourth Stokes parameters: neglected (expected near-zero for natural surfaces)
- Elevation angle effects on surface brightness: not yet characterized; future measurements over extended calm water needed
