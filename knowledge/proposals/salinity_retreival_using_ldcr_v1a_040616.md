## Executive Summary
This report presents a technical approach for high-resolution salinity mapping using the LDCR (Lobe-Differencing Conical Radiometer) instrument deployed on Black Swift Technologies small UAS platforms (Tempest or SuperSwift). The system addresses critical gaps in coastal and estuary salinity monitoring by offering superior spatial resolution (~100 m) and temporal flexibility compared to existing satellite sensors (Aquarius, SMOS, SMAP).

## Technical Approach
- **LDCR System**: Broad-beamed antenna mounted on sUAS at relatively low altitude to achieve spatial resolution comparable to sensor height
- **CU Lobe-Switching Concept**: Highly stable radiometer design with approximately two orders of magnitude more integration time than conventional spaceborne radiometers
- **Salinity Accuracy**: ~0.1–0.3 psu (practical salinity units) along flight tracks with 100 m resolution, contingent on surface infrared temperature measurement accuracy of ±0.3°C
- **Thermal Precision Requirement**: ~0.25 K per 100 m pixel, achievable with moderately improved thermal stabilization

## Use Cases & Applications

### Primary Application: Estuary & Coastal Salinity Monitoring
- **Geographic Focus**: Chesapeake Bay (example mission detailed)
- **Mission Profile**: ~8-hour total mission duration
- **Scientific Purpose**: Map salinity variability to understand:
  - Ocean circulation and heat transport (thermohaline-driven circulation)
  - CO2 uptake and ocean acidification
  - Estuary and coastal ecosystem biodiversity and mixing
  - Biological effects on ecosystem health and primary/secondary production
  - Harmful algal blooms (HABs)
  - Freshwater runoff dynamics and estuary mixing
  - Coastal sedimentation
  - Coastal currents and storm surge prediction

### Validation Role
- Provides independent high-resolution validation for satellite salinity missions (Aquarius, SMOS, SMAP)

## Key Results & Findings

### Performance Advantages Over Existing Systems
- **vs. Aquarius/SMOS/SMAP**: LDCR achieves ~100 m spatial resolution vs. 200 × 200 km satellite grid cells; data collection every flight vs. every 10 days
- **vs. Satellite Data Utility**: Satellite sensors are ineffective in coastal zones and estuaries due to land beam overlap and coarse resolution; limited for studying open ocean salinity variability at circulation boundaries
- **vs. Manned Aircraft**: sUAS deployment offers comparable or superior precision with lower cost and greater operational convenience

### Critical Capability Gap Addressed
The report emphasizes an urgent national need: only 6 operational salinity buoys service the entire Chesapeake Bay. Satellite programs (Aquarius, ESA SMOS) are costly and high-risk, with limited spatial/temporal resolution in coastal zones. LDCR on sUAS fills this gap with routine, affordable, high-resolution measurements.
