# Solar Airplane Concept Developed for Venus Exploration

## Document Metadata
- Type: NASA research concept/technical brief
- Client/Agency: NASA (Glenn Research Center)
- Program/Solicitation: Planetary Exploration, Aeronautics
- Date: 2005 (publication); document filed in 2018 BST SBIR Venus Phase I references
- BST Products/Systems Referenced: None (this is a NASA Glenn concept document, not a BST proposal)
- Key Personnel: Dr. Geoffrey A. Landis (NASA Glenn), Anthony J. Colozza (Analex/NASA Glenn)

## Executive Summary
NASA Glenn Research Center designed a solar-powered aircraft concept for sustained atmospheric exploration of Venus at high altitudes (65-75 km), where solar energy is abundant and atmospheric conditions are moderate. The aircraft would operate continuously above cloud layers or conduct multi-hour glide missions through lower altitudes to probe cloud layers and measure atmospheric, geological, and meteorological properties.

## Technical Approach
- **Power Source**: Solar cells covering wing and horizontal tail surfaces; solar flux on Venus is 2600 W/m² (nearly 2x Earth's 1370 W/m²)
- **Flight Altitude**: Primary operations at 65-75 km altitude where atmospheric pressure is similar to terrestrial flight altitudes and solar intensity reaches ~95% of exoatmospheric levels
- **Flight Duration**: Capable of sustained flight above wind speeds (~95 m/sec at cloud tops); battery storage determined infeasible for night-side operations
- **Deployment**: Aircraft folds for aeroshell delivery; wing hinges allow left/right panels to fold over center panel; tail boom hinges once to fold horizontal tail over wing top
- **Configuration**: Small aircraft sized for "Discovery" class scientific mission constraints

## Use Cases & Applications
**Venus Atmospheric Exploration:**
- Measurement of volatile inventory and isotope ratios to determine early atmospheric composition and water loss mechanisms
- "Sniffing" sulfur content to correlate with surface features (volcanoes, fumaroles)
- Measurement of infrared absorption and horizontal/vertical wind components across altitude, latitude, and subsolar longitude to understand atmospheric superrotation
- Dual-aircraft separation measurements for atmospheric correlation studies
- Characterization of cloud particles through chemical and physical sensors

**Venus Surface Exploration:**
- High-resolution radar mapping from 10s of kilometers altitude (vs. 100s km from orbiter) yielding 10x improvement in resolution with 100x reduction in required power
- Detailed study of specific surface features through hovering/circling capability
- Multi-aircraft constellation for simultaneous "snapshot" measurements of planetary climate

## Key Results
Analysis demonstrated technical feasibility of solar-powered aircraft operations at Venus altitudes above cloud layer under continuous illumination. Low-altitude glide-climb cycles identified as viable alternative for probing cloud layers despite battery insufficiency for night-side transit.

## Notable Details
- Venus exoatmospheric solar flux advantage enables solar power where Earth-based solar aircraft face constraints
- Atmospheric pressure at 65-75 km altitude (~0.1 atm) comparable to terrestrial aircraft operating altitudes, allowing conventional aerodynamic design principles
- Concept addresses fundamental Venus mysteries: atmospheric superrotation mechanism, volatile inventory evolution, surface-atmosphere coupling
- Awards recognition: ASM/AIAA Best Paper of 2002 for atmospheric flight mechanics
- Published in peer-reviewed venues (Journal of Spacecraft and Rockets, International Astronautical Congress)
