# The Intensity Forecasting Experiment (IFEX): A NOAA Multiyear Field Program for Improving Tropical Cyclone Intensity Forecasts

## Document Metadata
- **Type:** Research report / field program description
- **Client/Agency:** NOAA (National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration)
- **Program/Solicitation:** NOAA Hurricane Research Division (HRD) / National Hurricane Center (NHC)
- **Date:** Published November 2006 (in American Meteorological Society Bulletin); field work conducted 2005 Atlantic hurricane season
- **BST Products/Systems Referenced:** None (this is a NOAA research document, not a BST proposal)
- **Key Personnel:** Robert Rogers (corresponding author), and 11 co-authors from NOAA/AOML, University of Miami, and NCEP

## Executive Summary
IFEX is a multiyear NOAA field program designed to improve forecasts of tropical cyclone (TC) intensity by collecting observations across the entire TC life cycle, developing improved measurement technologies for real-time TC monitoring, and advancing understanding of physical processes governing intensity change. The 2005 Atlantic hurricane season was historic, with IFEX flying 45 NOAA P-3 and 48 NOAA G-IV research missions across 14 tropical cyclones, releasing nearly 2,500 dropsondes.

## Technical Approach

### Program Goals
1. **Collect observations spanning the TC life cycle** in various environments (genesis through mature to landfall/decay)
2. **Develop and refine measurement technologies** for improved real-time monitoring of TC intensity, structure, and environment
3. **Improve understanding of physical processes** important in TC intensity change at all life-cycle stages

### Data Assimilation Context
- Support development of the next-generation Hurricane Weather Research and Forecasting (HWRF) model (under development at EMC, planned operational in 2007)
- High-resolution coupled atmosphere-ocean-hurricane prediction system (~9 km grid initially)
- Advanced data assimilation and real-time observations for TC core circulation initialization
- Integration with NOAA Global Forecast System (GFS) model

### Observational Strategy
- Focus on **all life-cycle stages** (shift from previous 90% mature-hurricane-only focus)
- Collect **atmosphere and ocean** data in various conditions (shear, humidity, mesoscale ocean features)
- Provide **real-time transmission** of data via satellite communication (SATCOM)

## Products & Capabilities Described

### Aircraft & Platforms
1. **NOAA WP-3D (P-3) Aircraft** - Two research/operational aircraft (N42, N43)
   - Equipped with airborne Doppler radar, SFMR, dropsonde systems
   - 2005: 45 missions totaling 406 flight hours

2. **NOAA G-IV Aircraft** - High-altitude surveillance platform (N49)
   - 2005: 48 missions totaling 432 flight hours
   - Capability for high-altitude dropsondes (~200 hPa, ~12 km)

3. **NASA ER-2 Aircraft** - High-altitude remote sensing platform
   - 2005: 9 missions (part of NASA TCSP partnership)

4. **Naval Research Laboratory P-3** - Navy reconnaissance
   - 2005: 9 missions (part of NSF RAINEX partnership)

5. **Aerosonde Autonomous Vehicle**
   - First successful deployment into Tropical Storm Ophelia (16 September 2005)
   - Closest approach: 46 km southwest, 39 km northeast of storm center
   - Peak winds at 750-m altitude: 33 m/s and 38 m/s
   - Provided real-time wind measurements and high-resolution thermodynamic/kinematic data

### Key Measurement Technologies

#### **Airborne Doppler Radar**
- Provides three-dimensional wind and reflectivity fields within TC core
- 2004-2005: Developed real-time analysis system with:
  - Automatic quality control (QC)
  - Data dealiasing and motion correction
  - Two grid types: 
    - 2D vertical cross sections (1.5 km horizontal, 150 m vertical resolution)
    - 3D analysis near flight track (3-5 km horizontal, 0.5 km vertical)
  - High-speed SATCOM transmission capability
- Example: Hurricane Katrina analysis revealed asymmetric wind field with deeper strong-wind layers on east vs. west side

#### **Stepped-Frequency Microwave Radiometer (SFMR)**
- Measures brightness temperatures in microwave portion of spectrum sensitive to sea surface foaminess
- Estimates rain rate and surface wind speed
- 2004-2005: Algorithm refinement to remove low bias at high wind speeds (>50 m/s)
- Refined using concurrent measurements in category-5 Hurricanes Katrina and Rita
- 2005 deployment: Installed on both NOAA P-3s for operational use
- Provides surface wind estimates with less uncertainty than flight-level reductions and better coverage than dropsondes

#### **NCAR GPS Dropwindsondes (Dropsondes)**
- Record: ~2,500 released in 2005 (compared to 815 in 2003, 1,384 in 2004)
- ~1,000 from NOAA P-3s within 300 km of TC center
- ~200 in support of NSF RAINEX program
- New prototype GPS receiver (tested 2004, deployed August 2005 onward):
  - Success rate >90% reporting winds at standard 10-m height
  - Previous versions: >50% failure in winds >50 m/s
  - Improved capability for high-wind-speed measurements in eyewalls

#### **Real-Time Wind Analysis System (H*Wind)**
- Quality controls and objectively analyzes observations from multiple platforms
- Synthesizes data from aircraft flight-level winds, SFMR, satellites, buoys
- Displays experimental analysis products near-real time
- Example application: Hurricane Katrina analysis showed impact of SFMR data in reducing peak wind estimates from 55 m/s to 50 m/s, later confirmed by dropsondes and Doppler radar

#### **Airborne Ocean Profilers** (for oceanic interaction experiments)
- Airborne Expendable Conductivity-Temperature-Depth (AXCTD) probes
- Airborne Expendable Current Profiler (AXCP) probes
- Airborne Expendable Bathythermograph (AXBT) probes
- Used to document upper-ocean thermal structure and response to hurricane passage

#### **Satellite Communication (SATCOM)**
- Developed by NOAA/NESDIS and Remote Sensing Solutions, Inc.
- Peak bandwidth: 9,600 baud
- Enables transmission of large data volumes from aircraft in real time
- Critical for operationally providing data to NHC

## Use Cases & Applications

### 2005 Atlantic Hurricane Season Missions
**14 tropical cyclones/systems sampled:**

1. **Arlene** (9-10 Jun) - Early season Gulf coast TC; 79 dropsondes
2. **Cindy** (4-5 Jul) - Gulf coast TC; 30 dropsondes
3. **Dennis** (5-10 Jul) - Near-complete life cycle (tropical storm to landfall); 242 dropsondes; landfall experiment with mobile wind towers
4. **Pre-Eugene Wave** (14-16 Jul) - East Pacific pregenesis case; 126 dropsondes
5. **Emily** (16-18 Jul) - Inner-core G-IV testing; 123 dropsondes
6. **Franklin** (23 Jul) - Inner-core G-IV testing; 12 dropsondes
7. **TD 7/Gert** (23-25 Jul) - Wave to tropical depression to tropical storm; complete life-cycle monitoring every 12 h; 85 dropsondes
8. **Irene** (7-8 Aug, 12-13 Aug) - Saharan Air Layer Experiment (SALEX) case; 119 dropsondes
9. **Katrina** (24-29 Aug) - Rapid strengthening to category 5; pre- and postlandfall; 302 dropsondes; oceanic interaction experiment; devastating landfall
10. **TD 16/Ophelia** (6-17 Sep) - Life cycle from TD to hurricane to extratropical transition; 462 dropsondes; **first Aerosonde flight into hurricane on 16 Sep**
11. **Rita** (19-23 Sep) - Category 5; classic concentric eyewall; prestorm, in-storm, poststorm upper-ocean sampling; 503 dropsondes
12. **Wave** (27-28 Sep) - SALEX nondeveloping case; 61 dropsondes
13. **Wilma** (18-24 Oct) - Late-season intense storm; 308 dropsondes
14. **Gamma** (19 Nov) - End-of-season surprise; 22 dropsondes

### Research Experiments Conducted

#### **Frequent-Monitoring Experiment**
- Objective: Provide airborne Doppler datasets at all TC life-cycle stages for HWRF initialization and validation
- Flight strategy: Repeated every 12 h (depression to weak hurricane) or 24 h (mature hurricane)
- **Five TCs sampled for most/all of life cycle:**
  - Dennis (TS to landfall)
  - Gert (tropical disturbance to landfall)
  - Katrina (TS to landfall)
  - Ophelia (TD to extratropical transition)
  - Rita (TS to pre-landfall)

#### **Tropical Cyclogenesis Experiment**
- Study how tropical disturbance becomes tropical depression
- Focus on dynamic/thermodynamic transformations in low and midtroposphere
- **Two 2005 cases:**
  - Pre-Eugene tropical disturbance (East Pacific)
  - Tropical Storm Gert (sampled as tropical wave, developed in Bay of Campeche, made landfall as TS in southern Mexico)
- Measurements: Flight-level winds, Doppler data, dropsondes, microphysical probes
- Key finding: Transition from cyclonic shear vorticity in midtroposphere with no surface circulation (23 Jul) to closed mid- and surface circulation (24 Jul) documented

#### **Saharan Air Layer Experiment (SALEX)**
- Objective: Understand/predict how dry midlevel easterly jet and Saharan mineral dust affect Atlantic TC intensity
- Platform: NOAA G-IV at ~12 km altitude
- Strategy: GPS dropsondes at SAL boundaries and high moisture gradients
- **Four G-IV missions in 2005:**
  - Two missions with Tropical Storm Irene and SAL interaction
  - Two missions targeting tropical waves interacting with SAL
- Data assimilation: Humidity information assimilated into GFS model parallel runs

#### **Oceanic Interaction Experiment**
- Objective: Better understand atmosphere-ocean interaction during TC passage
- Focus areas: Ocean mixed layer response, warm-core eddies, Loop Current
- **Two case studies:**
  - **Post-Katrina (pre-Rita) sampling** (15 September):
    - Deployed airborne profilers over Loop Current and warm-core eddy
    - Found significant cooling (>5°C) in adjacent cold-core eddy
  - **Post-Rita sampling** (26 September):
    - Deployed 53 profilers at same positions as pre-Rita flight
    - Findings: <1°C cooling in Loop Current/warm-core eddy surface mixed layer
    - Net heat content loss ~10 kJ/cm² (comparable to previous storms)
    - Warm eddy translated ~120 km westward at ~12 km/day (vs. normal 3-5 km/day)
- Measurements: AXCTD, AXCP, AXBT probes; Air Force drifting buoys

#### **Landfall Experiment**
- Objective: Study TC kinematic structure before and after landfall
- **2005 cases:**
  - **Hurricane Dennis** (10 July): Two staggered research flights observing decay as it moved inland
  - **Hurricane Katrina** (29 August): Mission documenting wind field along coast and over Mississippi River delta
- Measurements: Flight-level winds, SFMR, dropsondes, airborne Doppler radar
- Collaboration: Mobile surface wind tower teams from University of Florida, FI
