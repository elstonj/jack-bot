# Structure of the Venus Neutral Atmosphere as Observed by the Radio Science Experiment VeRa on Venus Express

## Document Metadata

- Program/Solicitation: Venus Express (VEX) mission; VeRa (Venus Radio Science) experiment
- Date: Published 23 April 2009 (received 23 May 2008; revised 2 February 2009)

## Executive Summary
This paper reports on 118 radio occultation measurements of Venus's neutral atmosphere (40–90 km altitude range) collected by the VeRa experiment aboard ESA's Venus Express spacecraft between July 2006 and June 2007. The study derives radial profiles of neutral number density, temperature, and pressure as functions of latitude, revealing significant latitudinal temperature contrasts, distinct atmospheric stability structures, and a prominent "cold collar" region at high latitudes.

## Technical Approach
**Radio Occultation Method:**
- One-way, dual-frequency radio link (X and S bands: 3.6 and 13 cm wavelengths) stabilized by onboard ultrastable oscillator (USO)
- Radio signals transmitted from Venus Express and received by Earth ground stations during spacecraft occultation events
- Atmospheric refraction bends radio signal propagation; frequency shifts (perturbations as small as 3–4 Hz) indicate atmospheric path effects
- Refractivity profile m(h) determined via Abel transform from ray bending angles
- Neutral number density n(h) derived from refractivity using ideal gas law and hydrostatic equilibrium to calculate temperature T(h) and pressure p(h) profiles

**Data Processing:**
- Retrieved profiles cover altitude range 40–90 km (above this, ionospheric effects dominate; below ~32 km, atmosphere becomes too refractive for measurement)
- Upper boundary condition set at 200 K at 100 km altitude; profiles merge below ~80–90 km, making dependence on upper boundary negligible in target range
- Temperature uncertainty: ~1 K; pressure uncertainty: ~0.1%
- Data collected over three occultation seasons; 118 total profiles with uneven spatial distribution (southern hemisphere well-sampled across latitudes; northern hemisphere concentrated near pole)

## Products & Capabilities Described
**VeRa (Venus Radio Science Experiment):**
- What it is: Radio occultation instrument aboard ESA's Venus Express orbiter
- How it was used: Sounded Venus neutral atmosphere and ionosphere at 3.6 and 13 cm wavelengths via dual-frequency one-way radio link
- Key specifications:
  - Frequency precision: 3–4 Hz perturbations detectable
  - Altitude coverage: 40–90 km (neutral atmosphere)
  - 118 profiles retrieved over ~1.5 Venus years (350 Earth days)
  - Vertical resolution: high-resolution profiles with altitude accuracy <1 km
  - Spatial coverage: southern hemisphere uniformly sampled; northern high latitudes concentrated near pole

## Use Cases & Applications
**Scientific objectives:**
- Investigation of Venus neutral atmosphere structure (troposphere, mesosphere) as function of latitude, local time, and temporal variability
- Static stability analysis and identification of convective regions
- Tropopause altitude and temperature mapping
- Characterization of latitudinal temperature gradients and thermal inversions
- Study of solar-forced atmospheric structures (wave-2 tides)
- Comparison with previous Venus atmospheric measurements (Pioneer Venus, Venera, Magellan missions) and reference models (VIRA, VIRA-2)

## Key Results

**Atmospheric Structure:**
- Troposphere: surface (~735 K) to tropopause (~60 km), lapse rate ~10 K/km
- Mesosphere (middle atmosphere): tropopause to ~100 km, nearly isothermal to weakly stable
- Temperature range at tropopause: 210–280 K; large equator-to-pole contrast (~30 K at 1-bar level)

**Static Stability & Convection:**
- Highly stable atmosphere above 45 km
- Nearly adiabatic lapse rate in middle cloud layer (49–59 km), especially at midlatitudes
- Shallow convective regions (S < 0) only within middle cloud deck (~2–5 km at equator, ~10 km at poles)
- Two high-stability zones: above 64 km (upper cloud boundary) and just below middle clouds

**Tropopause Characteristics:**
- Altitude: ~59 km at low latitudes, ~62 km in cold collar region (55–77°), ~58 km near poles
- Temperature: maximum ~280 K at 17°N, minimum 218–219 K in cold collar (73–74°), rising to ~233 K at poles
- Pressure: 1000 hPa equatorially to 100–150 hPa in cold collar
- Inversion depth: 3–21 K depending on latitude; deeper in northern hemisphere cold collar

**Latitudinal Variations:**
- Temperature nearly constant equatorward of ±50° at constant pressure levels
- Poleward temperature gradient dominates above ±50° latitude
- Prominent "cold collar" inversion layer at 60–70 km altitude, 60–80° latitude, complementary in both hemispheres
- Wave-2 structure detected poleward of ±75° at 62 km altitude
- No significant long-term temporal changes across three occultation seasons

**Solar-Induced Structures:**
- Wave-2 solar tide signature at ~100 hPa (64 km) in polar region (±75–85°)
- Temperature amplitude: ~30 K with extrema at subsolar and antisolar points
- Other atmospheric dynamics (rotating polar dipoles, eddy motions, gravity waves) superimposed on diurnal effects

**Comparison with Previous Missions:**
- VeRa temperatures generally within 5 K of Pioneer Venus Orbiter Radio Occultation (PV-ORO) results
- Agreement with VIRA and VIRA-2 reference models typically <10 K at same pressure levels
- Largest deviations (20+ K) occur near tropopause inversion in polar regions
- Cold collar region measurements consistent across VeRa, PV-ORO, and Venera-15/16 datasets

## Notable Details

**Data Corrections & Improvements:**
- Initial analysis (Pätzold et al. 2007) contained errors in observational geometry affecting altitude assignments; corrected version reduces altitude differences between occultation seasons to <1 km (typical 0.2 km)
- Correction resolved unexpectedly large day/night temperature variations reported earlier
- Updated retrieval algorithms and reprocessing incorporated improved latitude assignments

**Atmospheric Composition:**
- Assumed 96.5% CO₂ and 3.5% N₂ for refractivity-to-density conversion constant C₁ = 1312 × 10⁻⁶ K·m·s²/kg
- Ionospheric contribution much weaker than atmospheric term below 100 km

**Mission Context:**
- Venus Express orbited Venus beginning April 2006
- Occultation observations occurred during three seasons of VEX nominal mission (July 2006–June 2007)
- Observations obtained across wide range of solar zenith angles (>90° for 50% of neutral atmosphere data), supporting systematic study of latitudinal, local time, and long-term variations

**Technical Validation:**
- Orbit reconstruction accuracy: <100 m along projected sky plane path; altitude uncertainties <100 m from orbit and retrieval errors
- Systematic errors from solar tidal distortions considered small but noted as needing further analysis
- High consistency between VeRa profiles across three occultation seasons validates data processing and retrieval methods
