# Analyses of in-situ airborne volcanic ash from the February 2000 eruption of Hekla Volcano, Iceland

## Document Metadata
- Type: Scientific research paper / peer-reviewed journal article
- Publication: Geophysical Research Letters, Vol. 29, No. 16 (published 21 August 2002)
- Related Organization: NASA/JPL and collaborators (not a Black Swift Technologies document)
- Date: Received 29 June 2001; Revised 17 December 2001; Accepted 19 December 2001; Published 21 August 2002
- Key Personnel: D. Pieri (JPL), C. Ma (Caltech), J. J. Simpson (Scripps/UCSD), G. Hufford (NOAA), T. Grindle (NASA Dryden), C. Grove (JPL)

## Executive Summary
This paper documents an unexpected encounter between a NASA DC-8 research aircraft and airborne volcanic ash from the 26 February 2000 eruption of Hekla Volcano, Iceland. Using SEM (scanning electron microscopy), EDS (energy-dispersive x-ray) spectroscopy, and FTIR analysis, the authors confirm the aircraft ingested volcanic ash and analyze the nature of the particles. The study demonstrates both the hazard volcanic ash poses to aircraft engines and the limitations of satellite-based split-window thermal infrared detection methods for identifying airborne ash.

## Technical Approach
- **SEM Analysis**: Electron microscopy at 15 keV using Camscan Series II microscope at Caltech with secondary-electron and backscattered-electron imaging
- **EDS Spectroscopy**: Energy-dispersive x-ray spectrometer to determine elemental composition of particles
- **FTIR Analysis**: Nicolet FTIR spectrophotometer for hemispherical reflectance measurements (2.08–15 μm wavelength) to characterize filter surface composition
- **Filter Sampling**: Analysis of Keddeg #22010 fiber filters from DC-8 air conditioning system (trap particles 1–10 μm)
- **Comparative Analysis**: Comparison of encounter filter samples vs. pristine unused filters and normal-use filters

## Products & Capabilities Described
**NASA DC-8 Research Aircraft**
- McDonnell-Douglas DC-8 used for scientific atmospheric research
- Main cabin cooling system with crescent-shaped intake ducts below nose (~1 meter rear of nose)
- Equipped with Keddeg #22010 fiber filters to trap airborne particles
- Equipped with on-board heated aerosol observation instruments and atmospheric chemistry sensors (SO₂, O₃ measurement capability)
- Suffered engine damage: all four engines sent to GE Kansas facility for refurbishment

## Use Cases & Applications
**Specific Mission Context:**
- SAGE-3 Ozone Loss and Validation Experiment (SOLVE) - Arctic ozone research
- Unexpected encounter occurred 28 February 2000 at 0510Z, altitude 37,000 feet ASL
- Aircraft position: 76°N, 00°W (approximately 700 nautical miles north of predicted ash plume position)
- Flight plan adjusted to avoid ash hazard but plume position predictions were inaccurate

**Applications Highlighted:**
- In-situ volcanic aerosol sampling for validating plume detection algorithms
- Aviation safety assessment of volcanic ash hazards
- Validation of ash plume trajectory models (Puff, VAFTAD, CANERM)
- Testing of satellite-based thermal IR ash detection algorithms

## Key Results

### Ash Composition & Characteristics
- Encountered particles: intensely aggregated shard-like particulates, primarily 1–10 μm size range
- Composition confirmed as andesitic basalt, consistent with Hekla volcano ash
- EDS spectra showed strong peaks for Si, Al, Mg, Fe, K, Ca, and notably **sulfur (S)** - the latter being distinctive for volcanic ash
- FTIR analysis indicated approximately 48–50% areal coverage of filter by andesitic basalt particles <75 μm

### Engine Damage
- Engine oil analysis (SOAP): elemental sulfur concentration ~500 ppm in all four engines (normal range <1 ppm)
- Bore-scope examination revealed:
  - Cooling holes plugged by melted ash
  - Erosion of leading edge coatings on turbine blades
  - December 1999 pre-encounter inspection showed no damage; only two incident-free flights before encounter
- Damage conclusively attributed to ash ingestion

### Filter Comparison
- **Pristine unused filter**: Only ~1 μm organic particles
- **Normal-use filter (3000 flight hours)**: Fine particles (<1–5 μm), uniform distribution, no aggregation, no sulfur signature, indicating non-volcanic background aerosols
- **Encounter filter**: Intense aggregation of larger (1–10 μm) particles with strong sulfur signature and volcanic elemental composition

### Remote Sensing Discrepancy
- AVHRR satellite data showed positive "split-window" thermal anomaly
- Conventional interpretation of positive anomaly = no ash threat (implies only water ice/meteorological clouds)
- In-situ data contradicted this: aircraft had actually ingested volcanic ash
- Likely cause: rapid entrainment of moisture caused ice coatings on volcanic particles as they entered stratosphere over Iceland
- Ice coating masked ash spectral signature from satellite detection but particles still posed severe ingestion hazard to engines

## Notable Details

1. **Unexpected Nature of Encounter**: Aircraft crew were aware of eruption and had adjusted flight plan to avoid ash; however, plume position predictions were inaccurate, placing actual plume 700 nm north of northernmost predicted position.

2. **Safety Implications**: The false-negative satellite detection is particularly concerning because:
   - Positive split-window anomaly implied airspace was safe when it was actually dangerous
   - Demonstrates split-window algorithm's failure to detect all dangerous airborne ash
   - Critical issue for heavily trafficked Arctic air corridors

3. **Ice Coating Mechanism**: Ice coatings on ash particles reduced abrasive damage to aircraft exterior but not to turbine engines during ingestion—revealing differential hazard profiles.

4. **Scientific Value**: Paper establishes in-situ aircraft filter sampling as a valuable validation technique for volcanic plume detection algorithms and trajectory models.

5. **Prediction Model Issues**: Incorrect altitude initialization of ash plume extent was the root cause of prediction failure; altitude-dependent wind shears (common in Arctic/sub-Arctic) amplify initialization errors.
