## Executive Summary
University of Colorado Boulder's Research and Engineering Center for Unmanned Vehicles (RECUV) will support Black Swift Technologies on a NASA SBIR Phase 2 project focused on advanced atmospheric sensing using coordinated unmanned aircraft. RECUV will conduct airframe/sensor design analysis, wind tunnel testing, and flight testing to develop and validate coordinated wind sensing capabilities using BST's Apollo platform.

## Technical Approach
- **Airframe Assessment**: Evaluate Apollo airframe performance capabilities and identify applications
- **Formation/Pattern Development**: Determine optimal aircraft formations and coordinated patterns to observe winds, turbulence, and fluxes
- **CFD Analysis**: Computational fluid dynamics assessment of modular nosecone and nosecone scoop designs
- **Sensor Design**: Design wind and PTH (Pressure, Temperature, Humidity) tests to identify sensor accuracy
- **Wind Tunnel Testing**: Verify PTH measurements meet specifications; test nosecone and sensor assembly
- **Flight Testing**: System verification at NOAA Boulder Atmospheric Observatory (BAO), Pawnee National Grasslands, and San Luis Valley

## Products & Capabilities Described

### Apollo Airframe
- Multi-aircraft platform capable of coordinated atmospheric sensing
- Performance capabilities being assessed for various applications
- Compatible with modular nosecone designs for sensor integration

### Sensor Systems
- PTH (Pressure, Temperature, Humidity) sensors
- Modular nosecone with scoop design for atmospheric sampling
- Nose-mounted sensor assemblies with scoop inlets

## Use Cases & Applications
- **Coordinated atmospheric sensing** using multiple aircraft
- **Wind measurement** and profiling
- **Turbulence characterization**
- **Flux measurements** (likely heat, momentum, or trace gas fluxes)
