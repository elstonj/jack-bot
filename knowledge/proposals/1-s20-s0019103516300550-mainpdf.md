# Variations of water vapor and cloud top altitude in the Venus' mesosphere from SPICAV/VEx observations

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a comprehensive analysis of water vapor abundance and cloud top altitude variations in Venus' mesosphere using 8+ years of SPICAV/VIS-IR spectrometer data from the Venus Express mission. Using the 1.38 μm water absorption band and 1.48 μm CO₂ bands for cloud detection, the authors retrieved global maps of H₂O mixing ratios and cloud structures across latitudes and longitudes, revealing latitude-dependent variations, longitudinal asymmetries correlated with topography, and stable conditions over the mission's timespan.

## Technical Approach

### Instrument & Data
- **SPICAV/VIS-IR spectrometer**: Acousto-Optical Tunable Filter (AOTF) spectrometer with spectral range 0.65–1.7 μm
  - Long wavelength (LW) channel: 1–1.7 μm, spectral resolution 5.2 cm⁻¹ (resolving power ~1400 at 1.4 μm)
  - Field of view: 2° (10 km footprint from pericenter; 2300 km from apocenter)
- **Observations**: ~4,600 nadir observing sessions (orbits 23–3147, April 2006–November 2014)
  - ~1.25 million averaged measurements processed for cloud top altitude
  - ~150,000 averaged measurements for water vapor

### Radiative Transfer Modeling
- **Method**: Spherical harmonic discrete ordinate method (SHDOMPP); plane-parallel approximation
- **Spectral line data**: HITRAN 2008 baseline; HITEMP2010 and CDSD2008 for 1.3–1.6 μm range
- **CO₂ broadening**: Air-broadened half-widths multiplied by 1.7 to account for CO₂ dominance in Venus atmosphere
- **Atmospheric profiles**: Venus International Reference Atmosphere (VIRA) model, interpolated to observation latitudes
- **Cloud model**: Three-layer simplified structure:
  - Upper haze (76–90 km): exponential extinction, mode 1 particles (r_g = 0.15 μm)
  - Upper clouds (57–76 km): exponential extinction, modes 1 & 2 particles (r_g = 0.15, 1.05 μm)
  - Middle/lower clouds (47–57 km): constant extinction, modes 1, 2, & 3 (r_g = 0.15, 1.25, 3.65 μm)
  - Particle composition: 85% H₂SO₄; refractive indices from Palmer & Williams (1975)
  - Aerosol scale height (H_a): Fixed at 4 km (range tested: 2–6 km)

### Retrieval Methods

**Cloud Top Altitude (τ = 1 level at 1.48 μm)**:
- Created look-up table of synthetic spectra using SHDOM for CO₂ bands (1.415–1.49 μm)
- Parameters: cloud top altitude z₀ (60–76 km, 2 km steps), scale height H_a (2–6 km), solar/observation angles (0–80°), azimuth (0–180°)
- Relative depth of 1.48 μm band used as retrieval parameter (ratio of signal at 1479 nm to continuum at 1468.25 and 1490.5 nm)
- Error: ±10–20 m (equatorial); ±200–300 m (polar regions)

**Water Vapor Mixing Ratio (1.38 μm band)**:
- Inverse problem solution using direct atmospheric model
- Three-step iterative procedure: (1) initial cloud albedo estimate, (2) continuum fitting, (3) golden section method optimization
- 16 adjacent spectra averaged (sliding average) to improve signal-to-noise ratio
- Assumption: uniform H₂O mixing ratio with altitude (sensitivity analysis in Section 5.5)
- Effective altitude of water line formation calculated for each observation based on Jacobian weighting
- Error: ±10–20% of retrieved value (0.5–1 ppm)

## Products & Capabilities Described

### SPICAV/VIS-IR Spectrometer
- **What it is**: Multi-channel near-infrared spectrometer on Venus Express with AOTF tunable filter and cooled detectors
- **H₂O measurement capability**: Detects H₂O at 0.94 μm, 1.14 μm, and strongest 1.38 μm band; sensitive to altitudes 55–70 km (vs. 65–70 km for 2.56 μm)
- **Cloud detection**: Uses CO₂ bands at 1.43 and 1.48 μm for cloud top altitude retrieval
- **Radiometric performance**: Noise-equivalent brightness 1.6 W/m²/μm/sr at 1.38 μm (cooled detectors; 2× higher before orbit 1500)
- **Unique capability**: Longest continuous middle-atmosphere H₂O dataset for Venus (8.5 years, dayside nadir observations)

## Use Cases & Applications

### Venus Atmospheric Science
1. **Water Vapor Distribution Mapping**: Global climatology of H₂O in mesosphere (59–66 km altitude range)
2. **Cloud Top Altitude Variations**: Latitudinal, longitudinal, and temporal mapping of cloud structure
3. **Atmospheric Chemistry**: H₂O role in sulfuric acid cloud formation and photochemical cycles
4. **Atmospheric Dynamics**: Detection of gravity wave signatures and topographic influences on atmospheric composition
5. **Thermal Balance**: Water vapor impact on Venus surface environment cooling

## Key Results

### Cloud Top Altitude (τ = 1 at 1.48 μm)
- **Latitudinal variation**: 
  - Low/middle latitudes (40°S–40°N): 68–73 km average 70.2 ± 0.8 km
  - High northern latitudes: 62–68 km
  - Highly symmetric between hemispheres
- **Local time dependence**: Small decrease (~1 km) from noon to terminators in Northern mid-latitudes; not observed in Southern hemisphere
- **Secular (long-term) variations**: **No prominent variations over 8.5 years** on any latitude bin (0°–75°)
- **Aerosol scale height**: Validated at 4 km using SPICAV solar occultation data (range 2.5–5 km observed; average 3.5 km at pole)

### Water Vapor Mixing Ratio (1.38 μm band)
- **Latitudinal distribution**:
  - Low latitudes (20°S–20°N): **6.1 ± 1.2 ppm** (range 4–11 ppm), effective altitude 61.9 ± 0.5 km
  - Mid-latitudes (30°–50°): **5.4 ± 1 ppm** (range 3–8 ppm, local minimum), effective altitude 62.1 ± 0.6 km
  - High latitudes (>60°): **7.2 ± 1.4 ppm** (range 3–12 ppm), effective altitude 60.6 ± 0.5 km
  - **Pattern**: Two maxima (equator and poles) with middle latitude minimum
  
- **Effective Altitude of Water Formation**:
  - Ranges 59–66 km depending on cloud top altitude and observation geometry
  - Strong correlation with water mixing ratio: increases from ~2 ppm at 66 km to 7–7.5 ppm at 59–61 km
  - Vertical gradient: ~0.8 ppm per km

- **Longitudinal Distribution (Asymmetry)**:
  - **Strong maximum**: 7–7.5 ppm from −120° to +30° longitude (shifted to 20°S–10°N)
  - **Minimum**: 5–6 ppm from +30° to +160° longitude
  - Minimum corresponds to **Aphrodite Terra highlands** (suggests topographic influence)
  
- **Local time variations**: **No prominent variations** (unlike cloud top)
- **Secular variations**: **No prominent long-term variations over 8.5 years**; variations within orbits ~1–2 ppm; orbit-to-orbit variations 5–6 ppm

### Vertical Distribution (from multiple viewing angles)
- **Constrained H₂O profile**: 
  - 2 ppm at 66 km altitude
  - 7–7.5 ppm at 59–61 km altitude

| Pioneer Venus OIR (45 μm) | 45 μm | 10–90 | 55–65 |
| Venera 15 FTS (6–40 μm) | 6–40 μm | 10±5 (low lat); 8±6 (high lat) | 62.5; 56 |
| VIRTIS-H (2.6 μm) | 2.6 μm | 3±1 (low lat); 5±2 (70–80° lat) | 68–69; 62–64 |
| **SPICAV-IR (1.38 μm, this work)** | **1.38 μm** | **6.1±1.2; 5.4±1; 7.2±1.4** | **61.9; 62.1; 60.6** |

**Consistency**: SPICAV H₂O mixing ratios higher than VIRTIS-H (2.56 μm) at 68–70 km altitudes, which is physically consistent given 1.38 μm band probes 10 km lower altitudes.

## Notable Details

### Scientific Insights
1. **Water vapor maxima at equator and poles**: Equatorial maximum supports upward convection and injection from lower atmosphere; polar increase correlates with cloud top altitude decrease and lower H₂O effective altitude
2. **Middle-latitude depletion**: Cannot be fully explained by vertical gradient of water within clouds; suggests additional transport or chemical processes
3. **Topographic signature**: Water minimum over Aphrodite Terra highlands; speculated connection to stationary gravity waves and vertical mixing

### Methodological Strengths
- **Multiple scattering treatment**: Full SHDOM radiative transfer accounting for Venus' cloudy, scattering atmosphere
- **Effective altitude calculation**: Jacobian-weighted method properly accounts for observation geometry and cloud top variations
- **Sensitivity analysis**: Comprehensive testing of aerosol scale height, vertical gradients, and retrieval parameter choices
- **Instrument characterization**: Detailed analysis of AOTF bandpass anomalies and instrumental function variations

### Data Quality & Coverage
- **Spatial coverage**: Extensive latitude-longitude sampling; special "zigzag" observations post-2013 for longitudinal coverage
- **Temporal coverage**: Nearly continuous 8.5-year mission enabling long-term stability assessment
- **Observation modes**: Mix of pericenter/apocenter passes
