## Executive Summary
The VOR MON Task Group reviewed and validated FAA selection criteria for reducing the national VOR network from 967 stations to approximately 500 by January 1, 2020, as part of the transition to NextGen PBN operations. The report recommends specific implementation criteria, a standardized decommissioning process, expanded VOR service volumes to lower altitudes, and emphasis on stakeholder communication and coordination.

## Technical Approach
The VOR MON Task Group conducted four-part work tasking:
1. **Task One (Completed)**: Review and validate VOR MON selection criteria and assumptions
2. **Task Two**: Review and validate draft candidate VOR MON list
3. **Task Three**: Review implementation planning and waterfall schedules
4. **Task Four**: Provide recommendations on outreach and education

The report focuses on Task One completion.

## Key Technical Requirements & Criteria

### Overall Program Objectives
- Transition from legacy network of 967 VORs to MON of ~500 VORs by January 1, 2020
- Maintain safety and coordination across FAA organizational lines
- Shift resources from legacy National Airspace System (NAS) to NextGen

### Coverage Criteria for Retention in VOR MON
**General Coverage:**
- Provide navigation coverage above 5,000 feet AGL (enroute coverage)
- Allow aircraft to fly VOR-to-VOR or to safe landing site with GPS-independent approach within 100 NM of any location in CONUS
- Support international arrival routes at Core 30 airports
- Support Hazardous In-Flight Weather Advisory (HIWAS) and Flight Service Station voice services

**Mountainous & Special Areas:**
- Retain all VORs outside CONUS
- Retain most VORs in western mountainous areas
- Maintain adequate coverage in known GPS jamming areas

**Enroute Coverage Enhancement:**
- Expand VOR service volumes to 70 NM at 4,000' AGL and 62 NM at 3,000' AGL (vs. current 77 NM at 5,000' AGL)
- Rationale: Allows aircraft unable to climb to 5,000' to remain out of icing layers or maintain separation in metroplex areas (e.g., Washington DC at 4,000-6,000 feet; Oregon coast icing conditions)

### DME/TACAN Retention
- DME and TACAN facilities will generally be retained (not subject to VOR MON reductions)
- If VOR service removed from co-located site, DME/TACAN retained
- FAA plans optimized DME network for DME/DME RNAV coverage

## Implementation Assumptions

**Reliance on VORs:**
- Civil aircraft GPS equipage rates (as of Nov 2012-Mar 2013): 86% air transport, 88% general aviation, 95% foreign carriers
- DoD will remain reliant on VORs for flight training, enroute/terminal/approach procedures for non-RNAV equipped aircraft
- Flight training will continue to use VORs for initial and recurrent requirements

**Equipage Considerations:**
- Mixed equipage will continue during transition to PBN
- VORs required for non-WAAS IFR GPS receivers (TSO-C129)
- VORs required for Part 121/135 dispatch of certain aircraft in specific circumstances

**Operational Reality:**
- DME/DME IRU aircraft will continue to operate and dispatch during GPS outages
- Once VOR MON implemented, will provide basic but less efficient terminal/enroute operations for VOR-only operators and GPS-equipped operators during GPS outages
- VOR MON not dependent on air traffic control surveillance technology

## Recommended Decommissioning Process

For each VOR proposed for decommissioning, FAA must address:

1. **Navigation Procedures:**
   - All STARS (Standard Terminal Arrival Routes)
   - All SIDS (Standard Instrument Departures)
   - All IAPs (Instrument Approach Procedures) including Missed Approaches
   - One Engine Inoperative (OEI) procedures
   - All Obstacle Departure Procedures (ODP) and takeoff minima
   - Holding Patterns, Preferred Routes, Fixes, Airways (High/Low), and VOR checkpoints

2. **Non-Navigation Services:**
   - Communications (FSS, HIWAS, ATIS)
   - Geographic position references (intersections, waypoints, airspace classifications, TFRs)
   - Sigmets/Airmets, PIREPs, NOTAMs
   - Inter-facility letters of agreement and MOUs
   - AeroNav Chart products

3. **Documentation & Coordination:**
   - Standardized "checklist" approach for all VOR decommissioning
   - Cost/benefit analysis including operator costs and inefficiencies
   - Coordination with user community
   - **Mitigation implementation should precede VOR shutdown** wherever practical

## Guiding Principles

- **Safety paramount**: VOR MON must meet target level of safety
- **Phased transition**: Allow reasonable time for aircraft equipage with GPS while accruing operational cost benefits
- **Backup capability**: VOR MON provides backup for GPS outages and non-GPS equipped aircraft
- **Training support**: Adequate VOR availability for civil and military pilot training
- **Standardization**: Standardized FAA approach to transitioning all navigation and non-navigation services
- **Transparency**: Robust, transparent process for public input on specific VOR discontinuances
- **GPS contingency**: Maintain availability in GPS jamming areas
- **Local ownership option**: Allow states/municipalities to assume ownership and maintenance of specific VORs to fill coverage gaps

## Key Findings & Recommendations

### Major Recommendations

1. **Implement standardized criteria for VOR retention:**
   - Proximity to GPS interference areas
   - Necessity for non-RNAV capable aircraft navigation
   - Training requirement support

2. **Expand VOR service volumes:**
   - Recommend 62 NM at 3,000' AGL and 70 NM at 4,000' AGL (vs. current 77 NM at 5,000')
   - Addresses icing layer avoidance and metroplex traffic segregation

3. **Allow non-federal VOR ownership:**
   - States/municipalities can maintain specific VORs to provide local coverage
   - Addresses stakeholder concerns about coverage gaps

4. **Support infrastructure:**
   - Allow local entities to assume ownership and maintenance responsibilities
   - Consider cost/benefit of continuation vs. decommissioning

5. **Stakeholder communication:**
   - Critical outreach and communications to user communities
   - Transparency into selection process essential for successful implementation
   - Address rationale for MON and mitigations for heavily impacted users

### Implementation Challenges & Considerations

- **Aggressive Timeline**: January 1, 2020 target date deemed "aggressive" given scope of rulemaking, policies, and procedures required
- **Equipage Diversity**: Mixed equipage reality during transition period; cannot wait for uniform GPS equipage before VOR decommissioning begins
- **VOR Recapitalization**: FAA ability to upgrade/maintain existing VORs affects timeline feasibility
- **Budget Constraints**: Funding availability crucial for full implementation; sequestration or budget cuts could affect timing
- **Procedure Complexity**: Example given: Victor 3 route extends Maine to Florida with 14 VORs identified for discontinuance; coordination with PBN route development essential

## Notable Details

- **Federal Register Timeline**: Initial VOR MON criteria published December 2011; clarified August 2012 with draft candidate list
- **Task Group Composition**: 27 members representing DoD, RTCA, airlines (Delta, Southwest, SkyWest), industry associations (NBAA, GAMA, A4A, ALPA), NATCA, NetJets, MITRE, Thales, and FAA subject matter experts
- **International Considerations**: Must maintain adequate coverage for international arrival airways at Core 30 major airports; international accessibility/utilization should not be adversely affected
- **GPS Equipage Context**: High civil equipage rates support transition, but DoD highlighted that many military GPS installations not designed for IFR approach phase operations
- **Alternative Navigation Research**: Separate efforts ongoing to identify Alternative Positioning, Navigation, and Timing (APNT) solutions for full-scale GPS backup system
