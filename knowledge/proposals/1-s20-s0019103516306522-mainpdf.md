# Discovery of a 150 Day Period in the Venus Condensational Clouds

## Document Metadata
- Type: Peer-reviewed scientific journal article
- Publication: Icarus, Vol. 286 (2017), pp. 118-133
- Authors: Kevin McGouldrick (University of Colorado Boulder), Constantine C.C. Tsang (Southwest Research Institute)
- Date: Published October 8, 2016 (received March 11, 2016)
- Data Source: Venus Express VIRTIS-M-IR instrument
- BST Connection: This document was filed in BST's Venus Phase I SBIR proposal reference folder (2018), suggesting relevance to BST's Venus exploration work

## Executive Summary
Analysis of 921 orbits (~2.5 years) of Venus Express VIRTIS-M infrared observations reveals a previously undocumented periodic oscillation in Venus's condensational clouds with a period of approximately 150 days. This oscillation is most pronounced at mid-latitudes (30°-60°) in near-infrared spectral windows (1.74 μm and 2.30 μm) and cannot be attributed to viewing geometry or instrument characteristics. The discovery provides new constraints on Venus's atmospheric dynamics and cloud vertical structure.

## Technical Approach

### Data Source & Instrument
- **Platform**: Venus Express spacecraft (polar orbit, periapsis 150-250 km altitude at +80° latitude, apoapsis ~66,000 km)
- **Instrument**: VIRTIS-M infrared channel
  - Wavelength coverage: 1-5 μm
  - Spectral resolution: λ/Δλ ≈ 300 (~0.01 μm, effective ~0.017 μm)
  - Spatial resolution: ~30 km at apoapsis, improving to ~5 km at 10,000 km distance
  - Detector: Mercury-cadmium-telluride (HgCdTe) cooled to 70 K
  - Data volume: ~1 terabyte over mission lifetime

### Data Processing
1. **Filtering criteria**: 
   - Integration times ≥0.3 s
   - Distance from Venus >10,000 km
   - Night side observations only (solar hour angle 5 a.m. to 7 p.m.)
   - Solar incidence angle >95° (tested at 100°, 105°, 110°, 115°)
   - Southern hemisphere only (due to orbital coverage advantages)

2. **Stray light correction**: Exponential curve fitting in "muntin" absorption bands (wavelengths: 1.05±0.01, 1.225±0.015, 1.38±0.03, 1.57±0.03 μm)

3. **Calibration**: 
   - Limb darkening correction using Longobardo et al. (2012) parameters
   - Spectral registration temperature-dependent adjustments
   - Odd-Even detector sensitivity effects evaluated via spectral integration

4. **Analysis methods**:
   - Linear trend fitting
   - Seven-day moving average smoothing (approximates global average given ~7-day cloud rotation period)
   - Least Squares Sinusoidal Fit Analysis (LSSA) for frequency spectrum determination
   - Period-folding analysis
   - P-value significance testing

## Key Findings

### 150-Day Oscillation (Primary Discovery)
- **Period**: 150.3 days at 1.74 μm; 150.5 days at 2.30 μm
- **Location**: Mid-latitude region (30°-60°)
- **Amplitude**: ~50% of maximum observed radiance; comparable to day-to-day variability
- **Characteristics**:
  - Broad peak (20-day half-width) suggesting quasi-periodic behavior or varying frequency
  - Not present in particle size parameter (Carlson ratio), indicating cloud vertical structure changes, not particle population changes
  - Decay and recovery timescales consistent with radiative-dynamical feedback
  - Absent at shorter wavelengths (less cloud-dominated)
  - Does not appear at polar or equatorial regions
  - Not driven by viewing geometry, spectrometer temperature, or instrumental artifacts

### Long-Term Trends
- **Global (hemisphere-wide)**: No significant long-term change in cloud cover over mission (essentially flat trend)
- **Mid-latitude region**: Slight increase in 1.74 μm and 2.30 μm radiance (indicating cloud thinning)
  - 1.74 μm: slope = 5.744×10⁻⁵ W m⁻² sr⁻¹ μm⁻¹ d⁻¹ (p < 0.0001)
  - 2.30 μm: slope = 3.652×10⁻⁵ W m⁻² sr⁻¹ μm⁻¹ d⁻¹ (p < 0.0001)
  - Total change ~5.3-5.6% over mission
- **Polar region**: Slight decrease in radiance (opposite to mid-latitude trend)
- **Equatorial region**: Slight increase in radiance

### Latitudinal Structure
- Local radiance maximum near 50° latitude (both 1.74 μm and 2.30 μm windows)
- Strong decrease poleward of 50° (dense polar collar cloud)
- Carlson particle size parameter increases significantly poleward of 50° (consistent with deeper cloud altitude at poles)
- Slight size parameter increase near 20° latitude (possibly Mode 3 particle convection signal)

## Use Cases & Applications Described

1. **Venus Cloud Monitoring**: Long-term tracking of Venus's condensational cloud cover and dynamics
2. **Atmospheric Composition Studies**: Inferring cloud vertical structure and particle properties
3. **Hadley Circulation Studies**: Constraints on meridional circulation (implied 0.9 m/s meridional wind if 150-day period driven by circulation from equator to 50° latitude)
4. **SO₂-Cloud Relationship**: Testing hypotheses about sulfur dioxide transformation into sulfuric acid clouds
5. **Wind Speed Validation**: Cross-comparison with ultraviolet wind speed measurements from other Venus missions
6. **Climate/Energy Transport**: Evidence for net equator-to-pole energy transport linked to observed atmospheric acceleration

## Notable Technical Details

### Spectral Windows Used
- **1.74 μm window**: Penetrates to lower clouds (50-55 km altitude), where bulk cloud mass resides
- **2.30 μm window**: Complementary observations for multi-wavelength analysis
- Both windows allow observation through CO₂ and H₂O absorption layers

### Calibration Issues Addressed
- **Odd-Even effect**: Non-uniform detector sensitivity between adjacent spectral bands; mitigated via spectral integration
- **Spectrometer temperature drift**: Verified not responsible for observed oscillations (no correlation with temperature periodogram)
- **Stray sunlight**: Minimal impact at wavelengths studied; exponential correction applied

### Cloud Altitude Constraints
- Condensational clouds: 50-70 km altitude
- Photochemical cloud source: ~63 km altitude
- Observable radiance primarily from 50-55 km region (lower condensational cloud)

## Hypotheses for 150-Day Period Origin

1. **Radiative-Dynamical Feedback**: Observed decay/recovery timescales consistent with cloud response to radiative forcing
2. **Hadley Circulation Modulation**: Possible coupling with meridional circulation, though implied wind speeds (0.9 m/s) seem low
3. **Momentum/Energy Transport**: Potential correlation with observed 10+ m/s wind speed increases at cloud tops (from other missions' UV observations), suggesting energy transport from high to low latitudes
4. **Atmospheric Acceleration**: Consistent with reported temporal increases in Venus wind speeds

## Uncertainties & Limitations

- **Data gaps**: Several weeks-long breaks in observations; uneven hemispheric and regional coverage
- **Instrument lifetime**: Only 4 Venus sidereal days (~486 Earth days each) observed; brief compared to possible long-term oscillations
- **Spectral artifacts**: Calibration uncertainties and Odd-Even effects still present despite corrections
- **Equatorial data quality**: Limited coverage, large emission angles, near-limb observations introduce larger uncertainties
- **Period precision**: Broad peak (20-day half-width) indicates frequency may vary with time
- **Regional specificity**: Unclear why 150-day oscillation appears only at mid-latitudes; polar and equatorial mechanisms may differ

## Future Work Referenced
