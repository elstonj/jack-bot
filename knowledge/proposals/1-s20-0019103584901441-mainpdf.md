# Microdunes and Other Aeolian Bedforms on Venus: Wind Tunnel Simulations

## Document Metadata
- Type: Academic research paper / journal article
- Publication: ICARUS, Vol. 60, pp. 152-160 (1984)
- Date Published: Received March 25, 1984; Revised June 16, 1984
- Authors: R. Greeley (Arizona State University), J. R. Marshall (University of Santa Clara), R. N. Leach (University of Santa Clara)
- Funding: NASA-Ames Aeolian Consortium for the Planetary Geology Program; Grant NCC2-60 and Grant NSG 2284
- Equipment/Facility: Venus Wind Tunnel (VWT) at NASA Ames Research Center
- BST Connection: Document filed in Black Swift Technologies 2018 NASA SBIR Venus Phase I proposal references folder (suggesting BST used this as background research for Venus science proposal)

## Executive Summary
This paper presents experimental results from the Venus Wind Tunnel (VWT) at NASA Ames demonstrating that sand can form dune-like structures (microdunes) under simulated Venusian atmospheric conditions. The study shows that aeolian bedforms develop at wind speeds below ~1.5 m/sec and disappear at higher speeds, which has implications for interpreting sparse aeolian features observed in Venera spacecraft imagery of Venus.

## Technical Approach

### Experimental Setup
- **Facility**: Venus Wind Tunnel at NASA Ames—closed-circuit, atmospheric boundary-layer tunnel (6 × 2.3 m dimensions)
- **Simulated Environment**: Operated at 30 bars CO₂ at ambient temperature to produce gas density equivalent to Venus conditions of 95 bars and 737°K
- **Test Material**: Quartz sand 50-200 μm diameter (mean 110 μm); preliminary tests with finer/coarser sand showed similar results
- **Methodology**: Test plate covered with ~1 cm loose sand layer; wind speed raised just above entrainment threshold; bedforms measured, photographed, and sketched after each run

### Key Physical Parameters Varied
- Wind speed (freestream velocity, u∞): 0.63 to 1.5+ m/sec
- Particle size: primarily 110 μm mean, with tests of wider ranges
- Run duration: 90 seconds to 5+ hours
- Atmospheric density effects on particle entrainment and ballistic behavior

## Products & Capabilities Described

### Venus Wind Tunnel (VWT)
- **What it is**: Specialized simulation facility for aeolian processes under Venusian atmospheric conditions
- **Operational specs**: 
  - Closed-circuit design with boundary-layer simulation
  - Dimensions: 6 × 2.3 m
  - Operates at 30 bars CO₂ to simulate 95 bar, 737°K Venus conditions
  - Primary factor: gas density equivalence (controls particle entrainment)
- **Use in this study**: Enabled controlled observation of sand bedform development under near-Venus conditions

## Bedform Types Observed

### 1. Microdunes (Primary Focus)
- **Characteristics**: Regularly spaced, well-defined structures with slip faces
- **Typical dimensions**: 9 cm long, 0.75 cm high
- **Wavelength-to-height ratio**: 24:1 to 12:1 (typical of terrestrial dunes)
- **Formation conditions**: Developed at wind speeds 0.63–1.07 m/sec
- **"True dune" criteria met**:
  - Asymmetric stoss (windward) slope (~6° from horizontal) with slip face
  - Slip face with gravitational slumping and sand creep
  - Low ratio of saltation path length to dune length (1:3 to 1:12)
  - Internal cross-bedding patterns parallel to slip face
  - Lack of particle-size sorting
  - Regular spacing with distinct flux oscillations ("smoking" crests)

### 2. Ripples
- Wavelength: 0.5–4.0 cm
- Height: few millimeters
- Correspondence to saltation path length
- Formed at wind speeds <1.0 m/sec
- Preliminary study; comprehensive results deferred

### 3. Waves
- Low-profile sand accumulations
- Formed at wind speeds >1.0 m/sec
- Wavelength up to 27 cm
- Intermediate transition between microdunes and featureless bed

### 4. Featureless Bed
- Wind speeds ≥1.5 m/sec
- Complete loss of bedform structures

## Key Results

### Wind Speed Relationships (Figure 7)
- **0.63 m/sec (near threshold)**: Microdunes with 18 cm average wavelength; small parallel ridges in troughs; minimal material loss over 5 hours
- **0.63–1.07 m/sec**: Wavelength decreases from 18 to ~8 cm as wind speed increases; microdune heights decrease
- **1.07–1.35 m/sec**: Progressive degradation; slip faces disappear; transition to waves (up to 27 cm wavelength)
- **≥1.5 m/sec**: Bed becomes flat and featureless

### Flux Behavior
- Microdunes show regular oscillations in sand flux corresponding to dune passage
- Well-defined perturbations ("smoking") at crest
- Minimal material transfer between structures (characteristic of true dunes, not ripples)
- High wind speeds increase flux dramatically: same sand volume removed in 90 sec at 1.25 m/sec as in 5 hours at 0.6 m/sec

### Internal Structure
- Steeply dipping cross-beds typical of terrestrial dunes observed in cross-section
- Bands represent former slip face positions
- Outcrop patterns on windward slopes consistent with dune mechanics
- Cross-bedding poorly preserved due to lack of particle-size sorting

### Saltation Characteristics
- Saltation path lengths: 1.5–4.0 cm
- Particles unable to "jump" to next bedform; fall onto slip face or base
- "Smoking" dune crests observed (particles streaming off in air)
- Wake flow: boundary layer separation and reattachment 2–5 cm downstream
- Reverse flow zone (2–3 cm wide) exhibits sporadic particle motion

## Physical Mechanisms Proposed

### Bedform Development Model
- **Perturbation mechanism**: Oscillations in friction speed (u*) and boundary-layer momentum
- **Initial formation**: Cyclic erosion-deposition patterns at bed leading edge propagate downstream
- **Low wind speeds**: Friction-speed troughs fall below saltation threshold → alternating entrain/quiescence zones → microdunes form
- **High wind speeds**: Both min/max friction speeds above threshold; particles saltate across quiescent zones → overlapping erosion-deposition → loss of sharp boundaries → slip faces disappear → transition to waves
- **Transition mechanism**: Saltation path length relative to bedform wavelength controls dune-to-wave transition; longer trajectories at higher speeds enable particles to bypass quiescent zones

## Implications for Venus

### Why Aeolian Features Are Sparse in Venera Imagery
1. **Wind speed sensitivity**: Bedforms only form at wind speeds <1.5 m/sec; brief gusts above this threshold destroy bedforms rapidly
2. **Weak preservation**: Internal structures (cross-bedding) poorly developed due to lack of particle-size sorting; difficult to detect in low-resolution imagery
3. **Small scale**: Microdunes are centimeter-scale features; unlikely to be resolved in Venera images
4. **Possible interpretations**: Some observed structures (e.g., corrugated depressions in Venera 13 images) may be ripple-like features or alternatively from sediment-gravity flows/volcanic eruptions

### Growth Potential
- Dunes observed to increase in size with time in VWT
- May grow to scales much larger than laboratory microdunes
- Capable of migrating in morphologically intact state across sand beds and solid surfaces
- Migration rates predicted to be much higher than terrestrial dunes or ripples

### Venus-Specific Conditions
- Extreme atmospheric pressure/temperature variation with elevation (45 bars, 650°K at Maxwell Montes; up to 107 bars, 757°K at lowest elevations) means aeolian bedform type and geometry may vary by location
- Bedforms may reflect historical processes when Venusian environment was different
- Study used representative case of 95 bars, 737°K

## Limitations & Future Work
- Limited particle size range tested; wider ranges planned
- Material composition: quartz used for ease (dynamic similarity to basalt assumed)
- Pumice sands not tested
- Effects of grain cohesion and shape not assessed
- Ripple study preliminary; comprehensive investigation deferred
- High wind speed bedform flattening mechanism unexplained
- Unknown whether featureless bed at 1.5+ m/sec represents true absence or wind tunnel artifact

### Data Points
- Wavelength range observed: 0.5 cm (ripples) to 27 cm (waves)
- Height range: few millimeters (ripples) to 0.75 cm (typical microdunes)
- Saltation path length: 1.5–4.0 cm
- Wind speed threshold for microdune formation: ~0.63 m/sec (just above saltation threshold)
- Wind speed threshold for featureless bed: ~1.5 m/sec
- Aspect ratio: 24:1 to 12:1 (wavelength:height)

### Conclusions
Experimental evidence demonstrates that:
1. Dunes **can form on Venus** but within narrow wind speed range
2. Venusian dunes may have scale of terrestrial ripples but with much faster migration rates
3. Evolution from microdunes → waves → featureless surface is controlled by saltation path length relative to bedform wavelength
4. Bedform development initially driven by boundary-layer momentum oscillations before slip face formation
5. Sparsity of observable aeolian features in spacecraft imagery explained by destructive gusts and poor preservation
