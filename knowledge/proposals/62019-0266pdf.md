## Executive Summary
This academic paper presents a theoretical framework unifying static and dynamic soaring by demonstrating that dynamic soaring can be understood as a transformed version of static soaring when viewed from a wind-fixed reference frame. The author introduces novel dynamic soaring parameters and shows that in steady-state dynamic soaring conditions, gliders effectively descend in a transformed gravity field with apparent weight inflation, making dynamic soaring analysis equivalent to familiar calm-wind gliding analysis.

## Technical Approach

**Theoretical Foundation:**
- Point-mass aircraft model with flat-earth assumption and no side-slip condition
- Aerodynamic model based on standard lift and drag equations with quadratic drag polar
- Wind fields modeled as purely spatial gradients (functions of height only, not time)
- Analysis conducted in both terrestrial and air-relative (wind-fixed) reference frames

**Key Innovation - Steady State Dynamic Soaring (SS-DS):**
Defined as conditions where in the air-relative frame:
- Airspeed Va remains constant (V̇a = 0)
- Flight path angle γa remains constant (γ̇a = 0)
- Heading angle ψa remains constant (ψ̇a = 0)

**Novel Parameters Introduced:**
- **WeightDS**: Vector sum of gravitational acceleration (g) and wind-reference frame acceleration (Ẇx), creating apparent weight inflation
- **θDS**: Angle between WeightDS vector and vertical gravity vector, defining rotated dynamic soaring reference frame
- **γDS**: Perceived/measured air-relative flight path angle in DS frame

## Products & Capabilities Described

While this document is not a BST product paper, it is referenced in a BST NASA SBIR proposal context:

**Application to UAV Systems:**
- Dynamic soaring algorithms enable extended mission endurance for UAVs
- Particularly applicable to small aircraft with meteorological data payloads
- Mentioned use cases: weather phenomenon observation, supercell structure monitoring, tornado formation observation

**Key Finding on Energy Extraction:**
- Even in steady-state dynamic soaring, net aerodynamic force exceeds aircraft resting weight due to reference frame acceleration
- Magnitude of net force: √(D² + L²) = m√(g² + (Ẇx)²)
- This exceeds simple gliding in still air where √(D² + L²) = mg

## Key Results/Findings

**Unification Principle:**
Dynamic soaring and static soaring are not separate phenomena but manifestations of the same underlying physics. When viewed from a wind-fixed reference frame, dynamic soaring reduces to descent in an apparent gravitational field with rotated orientation and increased magnitude.

**Mathematical Equivalences:**
- Maximum L/D remains unchanged regardless of apparent weight: (L/D)max = ½√(πAReCD0)
- Sink polar analysis applicable to dynamic soaring through weight-ratio scaling, familiar to competition glider pilots
- Temporal and spatial wind gradients produce identical aerodynamic effects on glider (glider cannot distinguish between them)

**Performance Implications:**
- Shallower achievable flight paths through wind gradients compared to still air
- Extended mission endurance through proper trajectory planning
- Higher minimum airspeed requirements (due to increased apparent weight)

## Notable Details

**Theoretical Contributions:**
- Novel presentation of three-dimensional sink polar surface (airspeed × weight ratio × sink rate) for dynamic soaring analysis
- Spatial/temporal gradient equivalence principle—gliders experience identical forces whether wind gradient is fixed in space or fixed in time
- Introduction of rotated DS frame makes dynamic soaring analysis tractable using familiar static gliding equations

**References to Black Swift-Relevant Work:**
