## Executive Summary
SPLASH is a comprehensive field campaign led by NOAA and research partners to deploy an integrated observing network across the East River watershed in Colorado (near Crested Butte/RMBL) between fall 2021 and summer 2022. The campaign aims to collect detailed observations of precipitation, surface-atmosphere exchange processes, cloud properties, and atmospheric boundary layer structure to improve weather prediction, water resource management, and understanding of hydrometeorological processes in complex mountain terrain.

---

## Technical Approach

### Overall Strategy
- Dense deployment of complementary observing systems (surface stations, remote sensors, uncrewed aircraft, radars) across multiple sites in the East River watershed
- Multi-scale measurements: from point observations (surface stations) to regional surveys (airborne systems)
- Data integration to evaluate and improve NOAA numerical weather prediction models (UFS, RRFS, National Water Model)
- Partnership with DOE Surface-Atmosphere Integrated Laboratory (SAIL) and Watershed Function SFA programs

### Key Observational Components

**Surface Energy Budget Measurements:**
- Atmospheric Surface Flux Stations (ASFS) measuring all components of surface energy budget: up/downwelling short- and longwave irradiance, winds, temperature, humidity, pressure, surface temperature, soil heat flux
- Data transmitted via Iridium satellite link; powered by methanol fuel cells (requiring re-fuel every 1-2 months) with option for line power
- Paired with solar-powered soil moisture sensor suites (depths: 5, 10, 20, 50, 100 cm), tipping bucket precipitation gauges, acoustic snow-depth sensors
- Plan: Two ASFS to be deployed (Avery Picnic area north of RMBL, and Kettle Ponds site)

**Radiative Flux Measurements:**
- Mobile SURFRAD: motorized tracking radiometer platform with 10-m tower hosting spectral radiation sensors (MFRSR), meteorological suite, total sky imager (TSI), laser ceilometer, TWST (cloud optical thickness) instrument
- RADSYS: smaller subset stations with shortwave/longwave irradiances, basic meteorology, ceilometer
- Both include instruments required for RadFlux processing algorithm

**Turbulent Flux and Boundary Layer Measurements:**
- NOAA Air Resources Laboratory (ARL) surface energy balance tower at 3 and 10 m heights with eddy covariance systems
- Temperature and wind profiles; Richardson number calculations for stability assessment
- Three downward-looking infrared radiometers for surface temperature
- Soil temperature/moisture profiles (5, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 75, 100 cm depth)
- Snow-ground interface temperature monitoring
- 4-component Hukseflux radiometer
- All-weather USCRN precipitation gauge with experimental Low Porosity Double Fence (LPDF) for solid precipitation catch efficiency
- Powered by 12 VDC lithium batteries (line power or solar charging)
- Plan: 1-3 suites to be deployed (Kettle Ponds super site, Pumphouse site, Brush Creek site)

## Products & Capabilities Described

### Black Swift Technologies S2 Aircraft

**System Description:**
- Fixed-wing uncrewed aircraft designed for aerial surveys
- Primary payload: passive L-band microwave radiometer for measuring volumetric soil moisture content (5-20 cm depth)
- Additional sensors: atmospheric temperature, pressure, humidity, and wind measurement instruments
- Provides high-resolution mapping capability

**Performance & Specifications:**
- Resolution: 10-20 m spatial resolution
- Coverage: Several square kilometers per deployment
- Operational timeframe: Late spring through summer months

**Proposed Use in SPLASH:**
- Deployment to multiple locations along East River to map seasonal evolution of soil moisture
- Comparable locations to HELiX multirotor platform surveys
- Complements ground-based soil moisture measurements from ASFS systems
- Provides spatial variability perspective to point measurements

**Advantages:**
- Novel observing platform for high-resolution soil moisture mapping
- Can be deployed to multiple locations across watershed
- Captures seasonal dynamics of soil moisture evolution

### Other UAS Platforms (for context/comparison)

**HELiX (Multirotor)**
- Gimballed multispectral camera (RedEdge MX) for surface characteristics and albedo mapping
- Up/downward-looking pyranometers (Kipp and Zonen)
- Vaisala RSS-421 sensors for thermodynamic profile measurements
- Maps surface albedo evolution during snow melt

**RAAVEN (University of Colorado)**
- Measures atmospheric thermodynamic and kinematic properties
- Turbulent flux profiles of heat and momentum
- Boundary layer evolution under varying wind/stability conditions
- Racetrack and profiling flight modes
- Summer deployment focused on convective precipitation processes

## Radar Systems

**Snow Level Radar (SLR)** – Two systems planned
- S-band, frequency-modulated, continuous-wave Doppler radar
- Mounted on boat-sized flat trailer (mobile)
- Measurements: vertical profiles of radar reflectivity, Doppler vertical velocity, snow-level height
- Operating frequency: 2.835 GHz
- Average power: ~1 W
- Applications: microphysics characterization, snow-level detection for NWS forecaster situational awareness, model validation
- Locations: Kettle Ponds site and south of Crested Butte Mountain (TBD)

**X-Band Scanning Radar** – One system
- Dual polarization parameters (reflectivity horizontal/vertical, differential reflectivity, differential phase, co-polar correlation coefficient)
- Doppler velocity and spectral width measurements
- Deployment on 8'×8'×10' seatainer (can be placed on ground surface or small gravel pad, no permanent concrete required)
- Average power: 12.5 W/channel; peak: 9 kW/channel
- Microphysical characterization of cold season precipitation
- Quantitative precipitation estimates for snowfall
- Identical to DOE SAIL X-band system for coordinated observations
- Location: South of Crested Butte Mountain along Highway 135 between Crested Butte and Gunnison

**Supporting Precipitation Instruments:**
- Parsivel optical disdrometer at each SLR location (hydrometeor fall speed, size distribution, type)

## Use Cases & Applications

### Primary Research Objectives

1. **Weather & Water Prediction Improvement**
   - Evaluate NOAA atmospheric models (UFS, RRFS) for complex terrain
   - Assess National Water Model (NWM) performance, including surface energy budget forcings and precipitation forcing
   - Evaluate land surface models in UFS and impact on mountain snow cover predictions
   - Support Colorado Basin River Forecast Center (CBRFC) and Grand Junction WFO operational forecasting

2. **Hydrological Understanding**
   - Evaluate forcing inputs to National Water Model
   - Understand surface energy budget controls on snowmelt rates and seasonal evolution
   - Assess soil moisture spatial variability and its impact on watershed processes
   - Support water resource management for Colorado River Basin

3. **Cloud & Precipitation Physics**
   - Cloud and precipitation vertical structure characterization
   - Microphysical processes in cold-season precipitation
   - Convective initiation over complex terrain
   - Cloud optical property retrieval improvements

4. **Boundary Layer Processes**
   - Cold air drainage events
   - Convectively-driven up-valley flows
   - Wind regime characterization for renewable energy development (wind turbines)
   - Atmospheric turbulence and aviation safety

5. **Novel Observing Platform Demonstration**
   - Validate utility of UAS (including S2 soil moisture mapping) for high-resolution weather prediction
   - Assess ASFS and advanced observing systems in operationally relevant scenarios
   - Demonstrate value for data assimilation into high-resolution numerical models

## Notable Details

### Campaign Duration & Location
- **Temporal Scope:** Fall 2021 to Summer 2022 (with option to extend through SAIL program lifetime if funding available)
- **Primary Location:** East River watershed, Crested Butte/RMBL area, Colorado
- **Multiple deployment sites:** Kettle Ponds "super site," Avery Picnic area, Pumphouse site, Brush Creek site, areas north and south of Crested Butte Mountain

### Partnership & Collaborations
- **Primary Partners:** NOAA (PSL, GML, ARL), University of Colorado Boulder, Colorado State University, Rocky Mountain Biological Laboratory
- **DOE Coordination:** Surface-Atmosphere Integrated Laboratory (SAIL), Watershed Function SFA
- **Engagement:** Detailed discussions with Daniel Feldman (SAIL PI, LBNL), Susan Hubbard (Watershed Function SFA lead, LBNL), Sally McFarlane (DOE ARM Program Manager), David Gochis (NCAR)
- **Operational Partners:** NWS Grand Junction WFO, Colorado Basin River Forecast Center, RMBL research community
- **Education & Outreach:** NOAA Hollings Scholars program, RECCS program, Gunnison Watershed RE-1J School District, 4-H, Girl Scouts

### Environmental Impact Mitigation
- **Minimal footprint design:** Surface-mounted systems, spiral anchors for ground stabilization, no permanent concrete pads (except consideration)
- **UAS safety:** Avoid overflight of populated areas, no fuel spill risk (electric-powered), fire extinguishers with operating teams, coordination with RMBL to avoid sensitive habitats
- **Radar RF safety:** X-band radar mounted on shelter top (eye-level minimum distance not an issue if not pointed downward); SLR operates at ~1 W average power
- **Soil disturbance:** Minimal; auger-based soil sensors eliminate need for large excavated pits; CSI SoilVue 10 self-contained probes used
- **Coordination:** RMBL Research and Animal Care Committee review of potential environmental impacts

### Key Watershed Context
- Colorado River Basin dependency on mountain snowmelt for water security
- Complex terrain challenges for weather/water prediction
- Projected runoff reductions 10-50% by mid-century due to climate change
- Orographic precipitation and boundary layer processes critical to understanding
- Renewable energy potential (wind harvest) in mountain terrain

### Importance of RMBL as Research Partner
- Co-location with DOE SAIL and Watershed Function SFA programs (comprehensive, coordinated observation)
- Existing partnerships with local community and land-owning entities
- Local expertise on watershed conditions and sampling strategies
- Existing infrastructure and research facilities
