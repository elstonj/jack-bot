# NightFOX SERTC Test Flight After Action Report

## Document Metadata
- Type: Situation Report / After Action Report
- Client/Agency: NOAA ESRL CSD (Earth System Research Laboratory, Chemical Sciences Division)
- Program/Solicitation: NOAA FIREX (Fire and Smoke Study) / NightFOX (Nighttime Fire Observations)
- Date: November 15, 2018 (report date); Test flight: November 8, 2018
- BST Products/Systems Referenced: S2 UAS
- Key Personnel: Ru-Shan Gao (NOAA), Troy Thornberry (NOAA), CU IRISS team (pilots)

## Executive Summary
A test flight of the S2 UAS with remote sensing payload was conducted on November 8, 2018, at SERTC facility in Pueblo, CO, to evaluate nighttime fire observation capabilities for the NightFOX project. While the mission was unable to observe the planned fire due to battery performance issues in cold weather conditions, the flight successfully demonstrated FAA-approved nighttime operations at 2000 ft AGL and validated the S2's lighting systems for night operations.

## Technical Approach
- Integration of remote sensing payload (infrared imaging system for fire detection) onto the S2 UAS
- Nighttime flight operations under FAA Certificate of Authorization (COA) with waiver approval
- Test flight planned at controlled burn site with wooden pallet and hay bale constructed fire
- Maximum authorized altitude: 2000 ft above ground level (still within line of sight)
- Flight conducted in severe cold weather conditions (near 20°F ambient temperature)

## Products & Capabilities Described

### S2 UAS
- Multi-rotor UAS platform modified with lighting systems for nighttime operations
- Capable of carrying remote sensing payloads
- Designed for UAS operations with in situ and remote sensing instruments
- Lighting system described as "clearly visible" and satisfactory for field operations
- Demonstrated ability to reach 2000 ft altitude with operational payload
- Right wing sustained minor damage from frost-related landing (remained flyable)

### Remote Sensing Payload
- Infrared imaging capability for fire/smoke detection
- Integration onto S2 successful based on prior Boulder test flights
- Objective: observe fire temperature and characteristics at night

### In Situ Payload
- Smoke plume sampling capability
- Not deployed in this test; planned for future laboratory fire sampling

## Use Cases & Applications
- Nighttime fire observation and monitoring (NightFOX program)
- Fire and smoke characterization during FIREX-AQ operations (planned for summer)
- Arctic operations and future NOAA ARL deployments

## Key Results

### Successes:
1. **FAA Authorization**: Obtained FAA COA for nighttime operations to 2000 ft AGL with support from Denver Approach
2. **Nighttime Flight Demonstrated**: Successfully conducted nighttime takeoff and landing with S2
3. **Lighting Validation**: S2 lighting systems proved adequate for nighttime operations visibility
4. **Maximum Altitude Achievement**: Reached authorized 2000 ft altitude with operating instrument payload
5. **Institutional Relationship**: Established working relationship with SERTC; future test opportunities planned for April

### Failures/Limitations:
- Fire observation not achieved due to operational constraints
- Smoke plume sampling not conducted
- UAS battery severely degraded by cold temperature (20°F), dropping from full charge to 70% during short flight
- Frost accumulation on aircraft during flight created icing hazard (pitco tube risk)
- Landing skid distance increased due to grass frost, resulting in minor wing damage
- Cancelled further flights due to icing conditions

## Notable Details

**Environmental Challenges:**
- Unexpected low temperatures (near 20°F) with unusually high relative humidity
- Frost formation on grass and aircraft wings during flight
- Brush at facility too damp for self-propagating fire; artificial fire constructed instead

**Operational Constraints:**
- Limited testing window due to: FAA COA/waiver processing time, CU IRISS crew duty time limits, site access legal negotiations, NOAA purchase order processing
- Crew duty time limit caused late afternoon arrival, reducing available test window

**Lessons Learned:**
1. Lighting systems critical for nighttime takeoff and landing safety
2. Adequate crew staffing necessary for desired operational tempo
3. Cold weather battery performance severely compromised; needs further investigation for operational limitation determination (noted as non-issue for FIREX-AQ in August, but important for future projects)

**Future Plans:**
- Advance instruments/payloads from prototype to operational status
- Laboratory fire sampling for in situ payload validation
- Proceed with FIREX-AQ deployment planned for summer operations
