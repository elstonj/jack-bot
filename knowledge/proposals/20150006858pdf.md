## Executive Summary
This NASA Langley Research Center technical paper presents trajectory design and analysis for the High Altitude Venus Operations Concept (HAVOC), a lighter-than-air mission architecture for exploring Venus's atmosphere. The paper describes aerocapture, entry, descent, and airship inflation trajectories for both unmanned robotic and crewed missions. Analysis concludes that an unmanned robotic Venus mission is feasible using current technology, while the crewed mission requires advanced aerodynamic decelerator technology.

## Technical Approach

### Overall Architecture
- **Vehicle Type**: Lighter-than-air (LTA) airships operating at ~50 km altitude
- **Mission Phases**: 
  1. Aerocapture (10-12 km/s → 7 km/s orbital velocity)
  2. Entry, Descent, Inflation (EDI) from 200 km to 50 km altitude
  3. Terminal descent and airship inflation/operations

### Key Technical Details

**Trajectory Design Tools:**
- POST2 (Program to Optimize Simulated Trajectories II) for 3-degree-of-freedom simulations
- CBAERO for aerodynamic/aeroheating database generation
- LAURA and OVERFLOW CFD codes for point-check validation
- VenusGRAM 2005 for atmospheric modeling (no winds assumed)
- Custom HAVOC-TDM (Terminal Descent Model) for inflation and descent analysis
- SolFlyte-LTA sizing utility for airship design

**Vehicle Configuration:**
- Axisymmetric "ellipsled" design: right circular cylinder with hemispherical nose cap and flat base
- Robotic version: 4.7 m diameter, 14.1 m length, 4,537 kg entry mass
- Manned version: 10.0 m diameter, 30.0 m length, 133.9 metric tons entry mass
- Crewed missions employ two separate vehicles (crew + airship/cargo) that rendezvous in orbit

**Aerodynamics:**
- Lift-to-drag ratio: 0.52 (both missions)
- Bank angle modulation used to control trajectory and mitigate lofting and g-loads
- Entry flight path angles: -4.75° (robotic), -4.25° (manned)
- Total angle of attack: 0-60°, with peak lift at 55°

**Venus Atmosphere Model:**
- At 50 km altitude: 1.5948 kg/m³ density, 106,679 Pa pressure (1.0528 atm), 350 K temperature
- Composition: ~97% CO₂, 3% N₂ (molecular weight 43.58 g/mol)
- Lifting gas: helium (4.0 g/mol) for airship buoyancy

## Products & Capabilities Described

### Thermal Protection System (TPS)
Two TPS candidates evaluated:

**HEEET (Heatshield for Extreme Entry Environment Technology)**
- Dual-layer: high-density outer "recession" layer + lower-density "insulation" layer
- 3D woven carbon fibers infused with phenolic resin
- Interface temperature limit: 315°C
- Low TRL but under development; expected to handle dual heat pulses with cold soak
- Higher mass but handles higher peak heat fluxes (~1,000 W/cm²)
- **Robotic mass**: ~3,918 kg (constant thickness), 3,527 kg (moderate tailoring), 1,775 kg (aggressive)
- **Manned mass**: ~30,646 kg (constant), 28,708 kg (moderate), 25,066 kg (aggressive)

**PICA (Phenolic Impregnated Carbon Ablator)**
- Monolithic resin-infused fiberform insulation
- Requires strain isolation pad (SIP)
- Interface temperature limit: 250°C
- Lower mass for lower heat flux environments
- Dual-pulse capability unverified
- **Robotic mass**: ~2,238 kg (constant), 2,125 kg (moderate), 1,329 kg (aggressive)
- **Manned mass**: ~14,151 kg (constant), 13,536 kg (moderate), 11,813 kg (aggressive)

**Recommendation**: PICA for robotic mission (lower mass); HEEET for manned mission (better high-heat performance, structural strength).

### Entry Vehicle Aeroshells
- Support structure: aluminum honeycomb (robotic) or titanium honeycomb (manned)
- Graphite/BMI facesheets
- Three TPS sizing approaches: constant thickness, moderate tailoring, aggressive tailoring

### Airship Design
Specifications derived using SolFlyte-LTA sizing utility, adapted for Venus:
- **Robotic airship**:
  - Volume: 1,118 m³ (packed: 20 m³)
  - Mass: 1,382 kg (including lifting gas and tanks)
  - Parachute diameter: 10 m
  - Inflation rate: 1 m³/s

- **Manned airship**:
  - Volume: 77,521 m³ (packed: 1,258 m³)
  - Mass: 95,776 kg (including lifting gas and tanks)
  - Parachute diameter: 24 m
  - Inflation rate: 400 m³/s

**Lifting Gas Requirement**: Crewed mission requires 8,200 kg of helium (alone) or 59,400 kg of life-support air mixture—major payload constraint for Mars-to-Venus transit.

### Aerodynamic Decelerators
- **Primary**: Disc-gap-band (DGB) supersonic parachute
  - Deployment window: Mach 2.25-1.00, dynamic pressure 900-200 Pa
  - Nominal deployment: Mach 2.1
  - Drag coefficients: 0.9 (supersonic), 0.85 (transonic), 1.15 (subsonic)

## Use Cases & Applications

### Primary Mission: Venus Atmospheric Exploration
- **Science Objectives**: Answer planetary science questions only accessible from atmospheric operations (per VEXAG and Decadal Survey)
- **Advantages over Mars missions**:
  - Shorter transit time: ~100 days outbound, ~300 days inbound (vs. ~1 year each way for Mars)
  - Habitable atmosphere at 50 km altitude: Earth-like temperature/pressure; radiation protection via atmosphere
  - Lifting gas (helium or air mixture) can serve dual life-support function
  
- **Unmanned Robotic Mission**: 
  - Feasible with current/near-term technology
  - Scientific payload deployment and sampling at 50 km altitude
  - Extended duration operations via solar-electric airship capability

- **Crewed Mission**:
  - Requires technology development (advanced parachutes/ballutes, dual-heat-pulse TPS validation)
  - Crew sheltered in pressurized airship cabin at ~50 km
  - Separate crew and cargo vehicles for launch efficiency

## Key Results

### Aerocapture Performance
| Parameter | Robotic | Manned |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Entry velocity | 11.3 km/s | 11.3 km/s |
| Target orbit | 300 km circular | 300 km circular |
| Peak g-load | 5-8 g | 6-7 g |
| Peak heat rate | 87-135 W/cm² | 273 W/cm² |
| Exit velocity | ~7 km/s | ~7 km/s |

### Entry, Descent, and Inflation (EDI) Performance
**Robotic Mission** (nominal case, moderate TPS tailoring):
- Entry flight path angle: -4.75°
- Entry angle of attack: 50°
- Peak g-load: 8.3-8.5 g
- Peak heat rate: 115-116 W/cm²
- Total heat load: 6,606-6,630 J/cm²
- Parachute deploy altitude: 82.5 km
- Parachute deploy velocity: 451-452 m/s
- Target operating altitude: 50 km
- Entry mass: 4,537 kg

**Manned Mission** (nominal case, from design space):
- Entry flight path angle: -4.25°
- Entry angle of attack: 50°
- Peak g-load: 6.7 g
- Peak heat rate: 273.65 W/cm²
- Total heat load: 17,760 J/cm²
- Parachute deploy altitude: 74.1 km
- Parachute deploy velocity: 486.8 m/s
- **Critical issue**: Dynamic pressure at Mach 2.1 is ~4.6 kPa, far exceeding the 900 Pa limit for conventional DGB parachutes—requires ribbon parachute or ballute technology
- Entry mass: 133.9 metric tons

### Thermal Protection System Results (Moderate Tailoring)
**Robotic Mission**:
- PICA recommended: 2,238 kg TPS + 1,031 kg structure = 3,269 kg total
- HEEET alternative: 3,918 kg TPS + 1,031 kg structure = 4,949 kg total

**Manned Mission**:
- HEEET recommended: 30,646 kg TPS + structure (PICA approaches heat flux limits)
- Aggressive tailoring can reduce HEEET mass to ~25,066 kg

### Trajectory Design Space Analysis
Valid unmanned trajectories identified with:
- Peak g-loads: 4.8-8.5 g (within crew tolerance for crewed mission if achieved)
- Peak heat rates: 87-135 W/cm² (robotic), 273+ W/cm² (manned)
- Total heat loads: 5,782-9,093 J/cm² (robotic), 17,760 J/cm² (manned)

### Feasibility Assessment
**Unmanned Robotic Mission**: **Feasible** using current technology
- PICA TPS is adequate
- Parachute deployment conditions achievable within DGB envelope
- All constraints (g-loads, heating, skip-out margins) satisfied

**Crewed Mission**: **Requires technology development**
- Advanced aerodynamic decelerator needed (ribbon parachute or ballute) to handle >4 kPa dynamic pressure at Mach 2.1
- Dual-pulse TPS capability (aerocapture + EDI heat) must be experimentally verified for both PICA and HEEET
- Guidance and control systems needed to optimize trajectory and possibly enable conventional DGB parachutes
- Lifting gas transport represents significant payload penalty (8,200+ kg)

## Notable Details

### Design Considerations
1. **Sharp Venus Atmospheric Density Gradient** drives major trajectory challenges:
   - Atmosphere skip-out risk
   - Excessive lofting (altitude oscillations)
   - High g-loads and heating rates
   - Shallow entry flight path angles preferred for g-load mitigation but increase skip-out risk

2. **Dual-Vehicle Crewed Architecture**: Crew and airship/cargo launched separately to manage mass; rendezvous in Venus orbit before EDI to optimize performance.

3. **Lifting Gas Transport**: Crewed mission payload severely constrained by need to transport helium or life-support gas mixture. Trade-off identified between pure helium (8,200 kg) and dual-purpose air mixture (59,400 kg for life support).
