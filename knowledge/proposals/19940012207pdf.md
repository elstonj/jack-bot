# Volcanic Thermal Features Observed by AVIRIS

## Executive Summary
This paper documents the use of the AVIRIS (Airborne Visible/Infrared Imaging Spectrometer) to detect and characterize thermal features at active volcanoes (Mount Etna and Stromboli, Italy) in July 1991. The research demonstrates the potential of imaging spectrometers to derive temperature distributions and thermal signatures of hot volcanic surfaces through spectral analysis and Planck curve fitting, with implications for future volcano monitoring systems.

## Technical Approach

**Data Collection & Processing:**
- AVIRIS flown over Mount Etna (July 19, 1991) and Stromboli (July 8, 1991)
- Radiometrically calibrated hyperspectral data in C-spectrometer (1.2-1.8 μm) and D-spectrometer (1.8-2.45 μm) bands
- 87 μs integration time per spectrum; pixel resolution ~320 m² IFOV (instantaneous field of view)
- Spectra corrected using atmospheric transmission coefficients (LOWTRAN7)

**Thermal Analysis Method:**
- Spectral radiance formula accounting for reflected sunlight, path radiance, and thermal radiation components
- "Difference spectrum" technique: subtract cool reference pixel from hot pixel, band-by-band
- Planck curve fitting using Simplex algorithm to extract temperature and emissivity from thermal component
- Excluded channels where modeled atmospheric transmittance <0.5

**Key Technical Challenge:**
- Spatial-spectral registration issue: sequential band recording (87 μs per spectrum) causes cross-track pixel displacement and spectrum distortion for small thermal features
- D-spectrometer had 7-fold throughput loss from faulty fiber-optics, but data remained interpretable

## Products & Capabilities Described

**AVIRIS Imaging Spectrometer:**
- Hyperspectral imager with 224 bands across 0.4-2.5 μm range
- Wide dynamic range (~20 mW cm⁻² sr⁻¹ μm⁻¹ for D-spectrometer)
- No sensor saturation observed on bright thermal targets
- Capable of detecting sub-pixel thermal features
- Records spectra 6 orders of magnitude faster than conventional field spectroradiometers
- Future improvement proposed: simultaneous recording of all bands (vs. sequential)

## Use Cases & Applications

**Demonstrated Applications:**
1. **Active Lava Flow Monitoring**: Detection and thermal characterization of lava-filled vents at summit craters
2. **Vent Characterization**: Identification of hot spots containing magma at or near surface
3. **Thermal Anomaly Mapping**: Detection of surfaces at magmatic temperatures in 1.2-2.45 μm bands

**Specific Sites Analyzed:**
- **Stromboli, Italy (July 8, 1991)**: Detected ~12 m² (3.72% of pixel) of ground at 598°C, consistent with recently erupted spatter or crusted lava in small intracrater bocca
- **Mount Etna, Italy (July 19, 1991)**: Characterized thermal signature of Northeast Crater with funnel-shaped pit emitting high-temperature gases and containing magma near surface

**Future Potential:**
- Hawaii lava flow fields and lava ponds (planned for November 1992 deployment)
- Refinement of eruption models through radiative property analysis
- EOS-era volcano monitoring applications

## Key Results

**Stromboli Analysis (Successfully Resolved):**
- Single Planck curve fit yielded 598°C ±0.95 for ~12 m² thermal component
- Emissivity approximately 0.95 (consistent with hot rock/spatter)
- Represents roughly 3.72% of pixel area

**Mount Etna Analysis (Partially Resolved):**
- Intense thermal anomaly from Northeast Crater detected
- Spectrum shape not readily interpretable as simple superposition of Planck curves
- Marked spectral drops at ~1.52 and 1.76 μm (potentially noise artifacts or genuine HCI absorption features; analysis inconclusive)
- Raw data examination recommended to exclude erroneous noise-spike corrections

**Performance Observations:**
- AVIRIS thermal detection capability confirmed for volcanic features
- Spectral resolution and sensitivity adequate for sub-pixel thermal anomalies
- Dynamic range sufficient (no saturation) for high-temperature objects

## Notable Details

**Technical Limitations Identified:**
1. **Cross-track distortion**: Sequential band recording causes ~1 pixel displacement and spectrum shape distortion for small features
2. **Interband spatial registration**: Different detector elements sample different ground locations; solution proposed by using neighboring detector arrays
3. **D-spectrometer degradation**: 7-fold throughput loss did not prevent thermal feature detection
