## Executive Summary
This final report documents Black Swift Technologies' SBIR Phase II-e work on developing a ruggedized UAS for scientific data gathering in harsh environments. The project focused on addressing limitations in atmospheric data collection for volcanic ash monitoring and other hazardous atmospheric phenomena by enabling small UAS to operate autonomously in difficult conditions without requiring extensive manual intervention and specialized expertise.

## Technical Approach
BST's approach centered on automating mission planning and flight management for small UAS operations in challenging atmospheric environments:

- **Autonomous Operations Focus**: Development of systems to conduct missions automatically in all-weather conditions, reducing reliance on highly trained teams and months of preparation
- **Real-Time Adaptive Management**: Implementation of software suite capabilities that can automatically observe wind conditions and adjust flight parameters to ensure adequate battery margin for safe return
- **Mission Planning Automation**: Reduction of manual flight planning burden that previously required months of preparation to account for communication constraints and rugged terrain navigation
- **Routine Capability**: Enabling the same level of operational capability that was demonstrated in Phase II-e to be performed repeatedly and globally without specialized expertise

## Products & Capabilities Described

### Small UAS Platform
- **Demonstrated Capability**: Previously achieved the only visual observations of target volcano during a calendar year during Phase II-e testing
- **Proposed Enhancement**: Automation of mission planning, wind observation, and adaptive flight management
- **Operational Constraint**: Prior missions required very knowledgeable and well-trained teams; proposed solution aims to democratize this capability

## Use Cases & Applications

### Primary Use Case: Volcanic Ash Monitoring
- Data collection for volcanic ash aviation hazards assessment
- In situ atmospheric measurements (wind, temperature) near volcanoes where ground stations are distant or at different elevations
- Validation data for atmospheric dispersion models used in volcanic hazard warnings

### Broader Applications
- Pollution alerts and toxic release response
- Dust storm monitoring
- Wildfire smoke hazard assessment
- General atmospheric model validation in remote/hazardous locations

## Problem Statement & Context
The document emphasizes critical safety implications:
- Existing atmospheric models (weather, dispersion) that inform public safety warnings rely on limited data sources
- Ground systems, crewed aircraft, balloons, and dropsondes have operational limitations
- Satellite data (ASTER, MODIS, AIRS, OMI) suffers from infrequent coverage, cloud masking, poor resolution, and validation gaps
- UAS theoretically can access airspaces too difficult or hazardous for crewed aircraft, but lack of all-weather autonomous capability prevents routine deployment

## Notable Details
- **Phase II-e Achievement**: Demonstrated the only visual observations of target volcano during an entire calendar year, proving concept feasibility
- **Operational Challenge Identified**: Despite proof of concept, the requirement for highly specialized teams and months of preparation severely limited practical deployment
- **Safety Case**: A key barrier to UAS adoption is the ability to automatically conduct missions in all weather conditions with appropriate safety margins
- **Geographic Scope**: Project intended to enable worldwide deployment of routine UAS volcanic monitoring missions
