# Influence of Venus topography on the zonal wind and UV albedo at cloud top level: The role of stationary gravity waves

## Document Metadata
- Type: Peer-reviewed journal article (research paper)
- Publication: Journal of Geophysical Research: Planets
- Date: Published online June 30, 2016; received October 16, 2015; accepted May 17, 2016
- DOI: 10.1002/2015JE004958
- Authors: Jean-Loup Bertaux, I.V. Khatuntsev, A. Hauchecorne, W.J. Markiewicz, E. Marcq, S. Lebonnois, M. Patsaeva, A. Turin, A. Fedorova
- Related to BST: Referenced in Venus Phase I proposal materials (filed in 2018 SBIR Venus project folder)

## Executive Summary
This paper reports a groundbreaking discovery that Venus's zonal (east-west) wind at cloud-top level (~67 ± 2 km altitude) correlates directly with surface topography despite a 60-70 km altitude separation. The authors demonstrate that stationary gravity waves generated by winds flowing over mountains (particularly Aphrodite Terra) propagate upward and break at cloud-top altitude, decelerating the zonal wind by up to 17 m/s downstream of elevated terrain. The study also reveals corresponding geographic patterns in UV albedo and water vapor distribution, providing new insights into Venus atmospheric circulation.

## Technical Approach & Key Findings

### Zonal Wind Measurements
- Data source: Venus Monitoring Camera (VMC) on Venus Express (VEX), 2006-2012
- Dataset: 31,604 manual wind measurements across 127 orbits in latitude range 5°S to 45°S
- Methodology: Cloud feature tracking in UV images at 365 nm (dayside only)
- Altitude of measurements: Cloud-top level at 67 ± 2 km (UV markings actually 65+ km)

### Wind-Topography Correlation
- **Key observation**: Zonal wind varies significantly with geographic longitude (5°S to 15°S latitude band):
  - Wind speed over flat terrain (200-330° longitude): ~101 m/s (retrograde)
  - Wind speed over elevated regions (60-100° longitude): ~83.4 m/s
  - **Total deceleration: 17.5 m/s**
  
- **Geographic shift**: Wind minimum is displaced ~30° downstream of elevated highlands (Aphrodite Terra and Atla Regio), with Aphrodite extending from 40-160° longitude at ~3000 m elevation

- **Correlation coefficient**: 0.805 maximum correlation when wind curve shifted 30° eastward relative to topography

- **Deceleration rate required**: 13.2 m/s/day (to account for 17 m/s change over 100° of longitude = 10,500 km at 95 m/s mean wind)

### Proposed Mechanism: Stationary Gravity Waves (GW)
The authors propose that orographic gravity waves generated by horizontal wind flow over topographic obstacles:
1. Form at ground level where wind encounters mountain slopes
2. Propagate vertically upward through stable atmosphere
3. Break at 50-60 km altitude (convective zone below cloud top)
4. Transfer momentum flux to mean flow, causing wind deceleration
5. Effect is stationary relative to ground/surface (not Sun)

**Physical requirements satisfied:**
- Static stability S > 0 throughout troposphere and up to ~55-58 km altitude (allowing GW propagation)
- Convective zone at 51-55 km has weak but positive stability
- Wind continuously increases with altitude up to 70 km, ensuring phase speed condition (stationary GW with zero phase speed relative to ground can always propagate)

### Supporting Evidence
- **VEGA-1 and VEGA-2 balloon data (1985)**: Measured at 53 km altitude
  - VEGA-1 (8.1°N, over flat terrain): 68.8 m/s average
  - VEGA-2 (7.5°S, over Aphrodite Terra): 65.9 m/s average (~4% lower)
  - Demonstrates GW effect already visible at 53 km (breaking continuing higher)
  
- **Previous GW observations**: Cloud features 200-1000 km in UV images (Mariner 10, Pioneer Venus); wave train detection 40-60° downstream of mountain peaks in polar regions (VMC/VEX)

- **Radio occultation evidence**: Temperature wiggles in Magellan and VEX radio occultation profiles (50-72 km altitude) consistent with stationary GW

## UV Albedo & Water Vapor Mapping

### UV Albedo Distribution
- Measured by VMC at 365 nm (wavelength of maximum contrast from unidentified UV absorber)
- High albedo: 0.52 over elevated terrain
- Low albedo: 0.43 over lower terrain
- Data averaged over 1442 images spanning 7.5 years (May 2006 to September 2013)

### Mechanism for UV Albedo Variation
- Related to horizontal wind divergence (dVx/dx):
  - **Wind stretching (dVx/dx > 0)**: 60° to -20° longitude
    - Compensatory upwelling of air
    - Transport of UV absorber to cloud top
    - **Lower albedo**
  
  - **Wind compression (dVx/dx < 0)**: 200° to 110° longitude
    - Compensatory downwelling
    - Less UV absorber at cloud top
    - **Higher albedo**

- Low-latitude meridional circulation also drives upwelling at equator with UV absorber brought upward

### Water Vapor Correlation
- Data from SPICAV IR (1.38 μm spectral feature) on VEX: 8.5 years, 150,000+ measurements
- **Striking anticorrelation** between UV albedo and H₂O mixing ratio in low-latitude regions (down to 20°S)
- Low UV albedo regions coincide with increased H₂O (>6 ppmv)
- High UV albedo regions coincide with decreased H₂O (<6 ppmv)
- H₂O acts as tracer of upwelling: strong negative vertical gradient (dμ/dz < 0) inside clouds

### UV Absorber Characteristics
- **Identity**: Still unknown; not SO₂ or SO (which absorb only shortward of 320 nm)
- **Candidates**: Sulfur allotropes (S₃, S₄, S₈), S₂O, polysulfur oxides
- **Correlation**: With SO₂ in atmosphere; major SO₂ located inside clouds (150 ppmv peak at 54 km per VEGA descent probe data)
- **Location**: Confined above 58 km but below cloud top (67 ± 2 km), produced within upper cloud and brought to cloud top by upwelling
- **Possible vertical distribution**: Well-mixed species between upper cloud bottom (~150 mbar) and cloud top, or thin layer at 70-72 km (above cloud top—less likely)

## Use Cases & Applications

### Venus Atmospheric Research
1. **Understanding superrotation**: Gravity wave momentum deposition mechanism must be accounted for in superrotation models
2. **General circulation modeling**: Current Venus GCM (LMD model) does not reproduce observed wind deceleration despite including orographic GW parameterization
3. **Temporal trend artifacts**: Explains apparent wind speed and UV contrast trends reported by prior studies (2006-2010) as aliasing artifacts related to subsolar point drift across geographic longitude zones of varying wind speed
4. **Cloud dynamics**: Explains longitude structure in UV markings and cloud-top features

### Comparative Planetology
- Demonstrates same mechanism (GW breaking) operates on Venus as on Earth mesosphere (Lindzen 1981 mechanism for terrestrial wind deceleration)
- Key difference: On Earth, GW propagation condition only met for certain wind speeds seasonally; on Venus, condition always met due to continuously increasing wind profile

### Altitude Profile Data
- Pioneer Venus probes: Wind profiles 0-90+ km; S > 0 throughout; weak values in 51-55 km convective zone
- VEGA-2 descent probe: S > 0 from 15-5 km; S < 0 below 5 km
- VeRa radio occultations: S > 0 throughout, very small only in 1-2 km bands around 51-54 km
- SO₂ vertical profile (VEGA in-situ): 150 ppmv peak at 54 km; <0.1-1 ppmv at cloud top (67 km); strong vertical gradient indicating destruction below cloud top

### Water Vapor Profile
- ~30 ppmv in bulk/lower atmosphere
- ~6 ppmv at cloud-top level (67 km per SPICAV IR)
- ~1-2 ppmv above clouds (per SOIR solar occultations)
- Model prediction (Krasnopolsky 2015): 25 ppmv at 48 km, 14 ppmv at 60 km, 4 ppmv at 65 km

## Notable Details & Context

### Methodological Insights
- **117-day periodogram peak**: Authors explain this reflects Venus solar day (time for same geographic point to face sun); proves wind depends on geographic longitude (reciprocal: if 117-day peak present, parameter likely correlates with geography)
- **Sampling bias correction**: VEX eccentric orbit (pericenter 200-400 km north pole, apocenter 66,000 km south pole) creates unequal latitude coverage; primarily samples southern hemisphere

### Connection to Prior Research
- Confirms intuition of Young et al. (1994): "large topographic slopes ubiquitous over Venus surface; topographic wave forcing may be important momentum source for global circulation"
- Updates understanding of VEGA balloon observations (1985) showing enhanced vertical winds/temperature excursions over Aphrodite region
- Resolves temporal trends in prior VMC studies as artifacts of geographic sampling

### Model Deficiencies
- LMD Venus GCM (Lebonnois et al. 2010) does not reproduce observed wind deceleration despite including orographic GW parameterization
- Authors suggest parameterization of GW and effect in deep atmosphere needs validation/improvement
- Model-observation discrepancy highlights gap in current understanding of Venus atmospheric momentum balance

### Future Missions
- Authors express hope that Japanese Akatsuki mission (successfully captured in orbit) will confirm and provide further detail on findings due to good imaging capabilities
