## Financial Health Assessment

### Summary
The **By Light Mustang project finished with a $100,000 (22%) cost overrun** against the $450,000 approved budget. Despite the overrun, all invoices were paid on time and in full. However, the project duration extended 26 days beyond the original 12/5/2025 due date, with cost growth concentrated in the Weeks 2–4 (MS1) phase.

### Key Findings

#### ✅ Strengths
1. **100% Payment Collection:** All $550,000 invoiced was paid; no receivables risk.
2. **Milestone-Based Invoicing:** Disciplined invoicing tied to completion milestones (10/9, ~11/3, ~11/24, ~12/17) maintained client alignment.
3. **Later Phases On Budget:** Weeks 5–9 phases remained within approved budgets; overage was isolated to MS1.
4. **Technical Success:** All major deliverables and flight testing completed; project archived as "complete."

#### ⚠️ Concerns
1. **Cost Overrun (22%):** $100,000 excess invoicing without documented pre-approval or change order. **This requires reconciliation:**
   - Was there a formal change order or budget amendment signed by By Light?
   - Did BST Finance approve the additional $100,000 in work scope?
   - Should the overage be absorbed by BST margin, or was it passed to the client as a change?
   
2. **Schedule Slip (26 days):** 
   - Due date: 12/5/2025; Completion: 12/31/2025
   - Weeks 1 and 2 experienced 37 and 23 days of slippage respectively, cascading through the project
   - Flight testing (Weeks 8–9) extended into late December/early January
   - **Root cause not documented in Asana or SOW.** Likely factors: technical challenges in integration/flight testing, weather, or scope expansion.

3. **Scope Ambiguity:** Two SOWs are referenced (one dated 10/03, one dated 09/30 with different technical approaches—SwiftCore vs. ArduPilot/Cube). Unclear which effort was invoiced and whether scope aligned.

4. **Missing Expense Detail:** 
   - Subcontractor costs (DSKinetic, Rocky Mountain High Aviation) not itemized in QuickBooks export.
   - No labor cost tracking provided; cannot validate whether the $100K overrun was due to labor escalation, scope creep, or subcontractor cost growth.
